Revelation Chapter 5

The Lamb and the Seven-Sealed Scroll – An In-Depth Study

Background and Context

Revelation Chapter 5 continues the Apostle John's vision of the heavenly throne room that began in Chapter 4. John sees God enthroned in glory, but now his focus shifts to a mysterious scroll in God's right hand. This chapter is a pivotal theological scene: it introduces God's redemptive plan (the contents of the scroll) and reveals Jesus Christ as the only worthy Redeemer to execute that plan 1 2. In Plymouth Brethren theology (a tradition known for a literal, dispensational approach to prophecy), Revelation 4-5 is viewed as a glimpse of heaven after the Church age has ended and before the great Tribulation judgments begin. They note that once the Lamb takes the scroll in chapter 5, the "woes" of God's wrath will follow - but the true Church (Christ's redeemed) is safely in heaven by then, not subject to that wrath 3. Chapter 5, then, serves as a heavenly prelude: the stage is set for God's judgments on earth (the seals to be opened in chapter 6 onward), yet in heaven we see worship, triumph, and the assurance that Christ will faithfully carry out God's purposes.

Understanding Revelation 5 is crucial doctrinally and devotionally. Doctrinally, it exalts Christ's unique qualifications as Messiah-Redeemer-King - He is depicted as both the "Lion of the tribe of Judah" and the slain Lamb who redeems mankind 4 5. It also highlights God's sovereignty over history: the scroll contains His decreed plan to judge evil and usher in Christ's Kingdom 6 2. Devotionally, this chapter invites personal application in our worship and faith. We see heaven's response to Christ - unceasing worship - and we are reminded that even now Christ is worthy of all our praise, trust, and devotion. Each verse of this chapter is rich with imagery, Old Testament parallels, and spiritual significance. In the study below, we will examine Revelation 5 verse by verse, drawing on parallel Scriptures and insights from Plymouth Brethren authors and like-minded commentators, to unpack every detail. May this deep dive not only increase our knowledge, but also deepen our personal reverence and hope in "the Lamb who was slain" and who now reigns in glory.

Revelation 5:1 - The Sealed Scroll in God's Right Hand

"And I saw in the right hand of Him who sat on the throne a book written within and on the backside, sealed with seven seals" (5:1). John's attention is captured by a scroll (book) in the right hand of God. God's right hand symbolizes authority and power - meaning this scroll carries the weight of God's own plan and power 1. Plymouth Brethren theologian John N. Darby noted that this scroll contains God's "counsels" - His decreed purposes to be executed by His power 1. Importantly, the scroll is written on both sides ("within and on the backside"), indicating that its message is full and complete nothing more can be added 7. This echoes Ezekiel 2:9-10, where a scroll "written on the front and back" contained divine judgments. In other words, God's plan is comprehensive; the scroll holds a complete record of what is to come.

The scroll is sealed with seven seals, signifying that its contents are perfectly sealed and hidden until God's appointed time. In John's day, important documents were often secured by seals to prevent unauthorized access 8. Notably, Roman law required wills to be sealed with seven seals, as in the cases of Emperor Augustus' and Vespasian's wills. By analogy, many interpreters (including dispensational teachers) see this scroll as something like the title deed of the earth or the inheritance of the Kingdom, which only the rightful heir can open 9. Indeed, one view holds that this scroll "reveals the judgments of God to be poured out on the earth, [leading to] Christ's Second Coming to rightfully inherit...planet Earth, and His righteous rule in the Millennial Kingdom" 9. In any case, the seven seals show that only an authorized, worthy person can break them. Until then, the scroll's contents (God's end-times plan of judgment and redemption) remain secret. This sets the dramatic tension: Who is worthy to break God's seals and unveil His plan?

For personal reflection, verse 1 reminds us that God holds history in His hand. All events - even the yet- future judgments and redemption are securely in His grasp and timing. Though we may not know the future details, we take comfort that God's plan is written and complete. He is in sovereign control, and His right hand will bring it to pass (cf. Isaiah 46:10-11).

Revelation 5:2 - A Mighty Angel's Challenge

"And I saw a strong angel proclaiming with a loud voice, 'Who is worthy to open the book, and to loose its seals?'" (5:2). Now a mighty angel steps forward as a herald. With a loud, commanding voice that echoes through heaven, he issues a challenge: "Who is worthy to open the scroll and break its seals?" This "strong angel" is not named - some speculate it could be Gabriel or Michael, given their roles in proclaiming God's messages (Gabriel in Daniel 8:16 and Luke 1:19, and Michael in Daniel 12:1) 10. However, the text does not specify, indicating that the focus is not on the angel's identity but on his authoritative question. The loud voice underscores the urgency and importance of this challenge 10. All of creation is put on notice: Is there anyone, in all the universe, qualified to enact God's ultimate plan?

The wording is crucial: "Who is worthy...?" Not just anyone can approach God's throne, take this scroll, and execute its decrees. The qualification is not merely strength or rank but worthiness - moral and spiritual fitness. This implies that opening the scroll is a sacred task that demands perfect righteousness and divine authority. As one commentator notes, the question implies a "formal challenge" that will display the glory and victory of the Lamb because it's setting the stage to show only One can meet these criteria 11 12. At this point, however, the question hangs unanswered, creating a dramatic pause in heaven.

Devotionally, consider the significance of that question for a moment: "Who is worthy...?" It highlights the utter inability of any created being - angel or human - to redeem creation or reveal God's mind by their own merit. It confronts us with the truth that no hero, leader, or angelic power is sufficient to fix what sin has broken. We are pointed to our desperate need for a Savior. This sets the stage for appreciating Christ's unique worthiness in the next verses.

Revelation 5:3 - No One in Creation Can Open the Scroll

"And no man in heaven, nor in earth, neither under the earth, was able to open the book, neither to look thereon" (5:3). A universal silence answers the angel's challenge. John observes that no one literally "no one in heaven or on earth or under the earth" - could step forward. This triple description (heaven, earth, under the earth) means no being anywhere in the universe was found able to open the scroll or even look into it. In other words, all creation is utterly unworthy and powerless to unveil God's secret purposes.

Not the mightiest archangel, not any saint or prophet, not any earthly king none can approach God's sealed decree.

This moment strongly underscores a doctrinal truth: the total inability of man (and even angels) to bring about God's ultimate redemption. It resonates with passages like Romans 3:10-12 ("there is none righteous, no, not one") no human qualification or effort can unlock salvation's plan. The fact that even heavenly beings are unworthy reminds us that the gap between Creator and creation is infinite when it comes to holiness and authority. The scroll contains God's sovereign plan, and only God Himself or one He appoints could rightfully open it. The stage is set for a God-Man Redeemer.

For us personally, this verse invites humility. If no one in heaven or earth could claim worthiness, we certainly cannot either. It reminds us that our salvation and the fulfillment of God's plans do not rest on any human ability. We cannot force God's hand in history; we cannot redeem ourselves or the world by our own merit. Apart from Christ, we stand in that same silence and impotence. Recognizing this prepares our hearts to fully appreciate Jesus's sufficiency and to give Him all the glory.

Revelation 5:4 - John's Weeping and Longing

"And I wept much, because no man was found worthy to open and to read the book, neither to look thereon" (5:4). At this point, John is overcome with emotion. He begins to weep profusely ("wept much"). The Greek implies loud, unrestrained weeping 13. Why does John weep? The verse says because no one was found worthy. John understands that if the scroll remains sealed, God's glorious plan of judgment and redemption would remain unrealized. All the promises of putting things right, vindicating God's people, judging evil, and bringing in the Kingdom - these "things which must be hereafter" (Rev 4:1) - would remain unknown and unfulfilled 14. In short, John weeps at the thought that God's plan could be stalled or lost if no Redeemer steps forward 14.

Some scholars suggest John's tears could also be for the sinfulness of man (that no human is worthy) or a fear that creation would never be restored 14. Ultimately, John's deep sorrow shows how precious the content of that scroll is - it represents the culmination of God's salvation story. If no one can open it, it's as if hope is lost and evil would never be overturned. John longs to see God's redemption plan unveiled; his weeping reveals a heart that yearns for God's justice and kingdom to come.

In a devotional sense, John's weeping challenges us: Do we also long for God's purposes to be fulfilled? Do we yearn for righteousness to triumph and for Christ to be revealed in glory? It is appropriate to grieve at the idea of God's justice being delayed - much like we pray "Thy Kingdom come" with earnest desire. Yet, as John is about to learn, God has provided One who is worthy. For believers, our moments of despair can turn to hope when we remember Jesus is able to accomplish all of God's plans. What John couldn't see for a moment (through his tears) is that Christ was about to be revealed as the Answer. In our trials, we too may weep; but we must "weep not" (v.5) as ultimate hope is found in the Lamb of God.

Revelation 5:5 - "Behold, the Lion of Judah Has Prevailed!"

"And one of the elders saith unto me, Weep not: behold, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, hath prevailed to open the book, and to loose the seven seals thereof." (5:5). Here the drama pivots. One of the twenty-four elders - representatives of God's redeemed (many believe these elders represent the glorified Church and Old Testament saints around God's throne 15 16) comforts John with good news: "Stop weeping! Look, there is someone worthy!" This elder identifies the worthy One with messianic titles drawn from Old Testament prophecy: "the Lion of the tribe of Judah" and "the Root of David."

"The Lion of the tribe of Judah" harkens back to Genesis 49:9-10, where Jacob prophesied that Judah is a young lion and the royal scepter will not depart from him. This became a title for the Messiah, the ultimate ruler from Judah's line 4. A lion symbolizes kingly authority, strength, and majesty - "king of beasts." Indeed, Christ, being from Judah's lineage (Matthew 1:2, Hebrews 7:14), fulfills this prophecy as the King with fearless power and dignity 4. Proverbs 19:12 says, "The king's wrath is like the roaring of a lion," and Christ will soon exercise kingly wrath against the wicked 17. Thus, the elder is saying: the mighty King from Judah's line has arrived.

"The Root of David" is another messianic title, from Isaiah 11:1,10, which foretold a "root of Jesse (David's father)" who would arise to rule the nations. It emphasizes that Messiah comes from David's dynasty (2 Samuel 7:16) and yet is greater than David, being the root/source of David's line as well as its offspring 18. Jesus later calls Himself "the Root and Offspring of David" (Revelation 22:16), meaning He is both David's Creator and also a descendant in David's royal line a mystery pointing to His deity and humanity. By calling Jesus the Root of David, the elder highlights that Jesus is the promised heir to David's throne, destined to rule Israel and the world (Luke 1:32-33) 18.

The elder then declares that this Lion/Root "has prevailed" that is, He has overcome (the Greek nikaō means to conquer or be victorious). Jesus won a decisive victory that qualifies Him to open the scroll 19. What victory is in view? The victory of His life, death, and resurrection. By His perfect obedience and sacrificial death, Jesus conquered sin, death, and Satan. Through the cross and resurrection, Christ triumphed (Colossians 2:15), thus earning the right to assume the reins of God's plan. The elder is essentially announcing the gospel victory: Jesus the Messiah has conquered; therefore He can enact God's final purposes. As one exposition puts it, Christ "secured the right to open the scroll based on His Messianic office, holiness, and redemptive death on the cross" 19. The Lion has roared in triumph, and the tears of despair can cease.

For believers, this verse is a burst of hope: "Weep not... behold the Lion!" It teaches us that the answer to our deepest longings and grief is found in Jesus. When we feel the weight of the world's evil or our personal struggles, we are invited to "behold" Christ - to gaze by faith on our powerful Lion of Judah. He has already prevailed at the cross, and one day He will enforce that victory over all the earth. Thus, no situation is truly hopeless. In our personal applications, when anxiety or sorrow weighs us down, let this verse encourage us to lift our eyes to the conquering Lion, Jesus Christ, who will finish God's work in righteousness.

Revelation 5:6 - The Lamb Who Was Slain (Seven Horns and Seven Eyes)

"And I beheld, and, lo, in the midst of the throne and of the four living creatures, and in the midst of the elders, stood a Lamb as it had been slain, having seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven Spirits of God sent forth into all the earth" (5:6). John is told to behold a "Lion," but when he actually looks, he sees a Lamb! This is one of the most striking and moving images in Scripture: the Messiah is both Lion and Lamb. The Lion denotes His kingly might, yet He appears as a Lamb that had been slain - denoting His sacrifice. These two pictures seem opposite (a Lion is strong and ferocious, a lamb is meek and vulnerable), yet in Christ they beautifully converge 19. As one commentator explains, "the symbols of a Lamb and a Lion...complement each other," portraying Jesus' "vicarious redemptive work" (as the Lamb who died for us) and His "victorious resurrection and rule" (as the Lion who conquered death) 19. Jesus won by apparent weakness - His victory was gained by dying 20. The Lion's triumph (v.5) came through the Lamb's sacrifice (v. 6).

John sees the Lamb "standing" in the center of the throne room, surrounded by the living creatures and elders. The fact that the Lamb stands "in the midst" of the throne and heavenly company shows that He is the central figure in heaven at the very heart of God's throne and purposes 21. Though Jesus is currently seated at the Father's right hand, John sees Him standing possibly indicating that the time for action has come. In Psalm 102:13 it was prophesied, "You will arise and have mercy on Zion, for the time to favor her has come." Indeed, Christ's standing suggests He is about to step forward to act in judgment and deliverance (compare Stephen's vision of Christ standing at God's right hand in Acts 7:56). As one Brethren author notes, "Now He is seated... but when the time comes for His enemies to be made His footstool, He will arise to act" 22. Thus, the Lamb is poised for the next phase of God's plan.

Crucially, the Lamb appears "as having been slain." The Greek term implies a lamb that had been slaughtered as a sacrifice 23. John sees visible marks or a form that shows this Lamb was killed - yet it is alive, standing in resurrection glory. Jesus still bears the scars of His crucifixion (cf. John 20:27), eternally reminding heaven of the price of redemption. The vision of a slain Lamb at the center of God's throne teaches a profound doctrine: Christ's sacrificial death is at the very center of God's throne and plan. All of God's purposes revolve around the atoning work of Jesus. It was because He was slain that He is worthy (v.9). What seemed like His defeat (death) was in truth His pathway to ultimate authority - "the Lamb slain is the Lion" who now will have victory over all enemies 20.

The Lamb has some unusual features: "seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven Spirits of God sent out into all the earth." These are symbolic attributes. In biblical imagery, horns represent strength or power (e.g. Psalm 89:17, Daniel 7:24). Seven horns indicate perfect or complete power - omnipotence. Eyes represent sight or knowledge; seven eyes indicate perfect knowledge and insight - omniscience. The text equates the seven eyes with "the seven Spirits of God sent out into all the earth," an allusion to the fullness of the Holy Spirit (see Revelation 1:4, 4:5) in His omnipresent activity. This likely echoes Zechariah 4:10, which describes "the seven eyes of the LORD which range through the whole earth." Thus, in the Lamb we see the fullness of the Holy Spirit's power and vision. One commentator writes that this portrays Christ "in the fullness of His omnipotence, omniscience, and omnipresence" 24. In short, the Lamb has all the attributes of God - He possesses all power and the all-seeing Spirit of God. This is a strong affirmation of Christ's deity and sovereign authority.

Plymouth Brethren expositors also note how the number seven appears three times in this scene (seven seals, seven horns, seven eyes), emphasizing the perfection of Christ's qualifications 25. He is completely worthy in every aspect - morally (His sacrifice), royally (His power to rule), and spiritually (His divine Spirit). For our personal walk, this vision of Jesus as the Lamb slain yet powerful is deeply encouraging. It reminds us that our Lord is the Lamb who died for our sins - tender and merciful - but He is also the mighty Horn of our salvation (Luke 1:69) who is able to save and to reign. Whenever we doubt Christ's care, we remember His scars; whenever we doubt His ability, we remember His horns and eyes. Our Savior is both loving and all-powerful. We can trust Him fully with our salvation and the outcome of history.

Revelation 5:7 - The Lamb Takes the Scroll

"And He came and took the book out of the right hand of Him that sat upon the throne" (5:7). In a dramatic moment, the Lamb steps up and takes the scroll from God's own hand. This simple action is packed with significance. No angel or elder dared approach, but Jesus, the Son of God, can approach the Father's throne with authority. By taking the scroll, Christ asserts His right to enact God's plan. It fulfills the vision of Daniel 7:13-14, where one "like a Son of Man" comes before the Ancient of Days to receive dominion and a kingdom. Here, the Lamb - who is also the Son of Man and Son of God - formally receives the divine plan to execute it.

This transfer of the scroll signifies a crucial "administrative change" in God's dealings. Previously, God's judgments on earth were often carried out by angels (as executors of providence) 26 27. But now that the Lamb has triumphed and been glorified, authority shifts to Christ and His redeemed. Brethren writers point out that in Revelation 4, angels were not distinguished from the "living creatures" (cherubim) because angels were the agents of God's governing work. But in Revelation 5, once the Lamb takes the book, the roles change - the redeemed saints (symbolized by the elders and perhaps now also in union with the living creatures) are to be given authority in the coming Kingdom, and the angels step back 26 27. As Hebrews 2:5 says, "the world to come" will not be in subjection to angels, but to Christ and those who reign with Him. Thus, when Jesus takes the scroll, heaven shifts its focus - the Lamb, not angels, will open the seals, and His people will eventually rule with Him. William Kelly explains that "the assumption of the book...marks a change of administration. Up to that point...angels have held an executory ministry...but... the world to come [will be under] Christ and those that are His in heavenly glory" 26 27. It's a beautiful picture of how Christ's victory elevates redeemed humans to participate in His reign (a truth we'll see in verses 9-10).

By taking the scroll, Jesus also signals that the time has come to reveal and enact its contents. All heaven has been awaiting this moment. There is a sense of momentous relief at last, the rightful Redeemer has stepped forward! From this point on, the Lamb will begin opening the seals (chapter 6 and onward), which will unleash the events of the Tribulation leading to His second coming.

In personal terms, consider what this means: Jesus holds the future in His hands. When He takes that scroll, it's as if to say, "I will now take responsibility to cleanse the world of evil and bring in righteousness." Our lives, too, are in His faithful hands. We can trust that He will move history - and our personal story - toward God's good ends. No force can snatch the scroll from Him. Just as none but Christ could take it, so none can stop Him from fulfilling it. This gives us great confidence in our Lord.

Revelation 5:8 - Worship: Harps and Bowls of Prayers

"And when He had taken the book, the four living creatures and twenty-four elders fell down before the Lamb, having every one of them harps, and golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of saints" (5:8). The instant the Lamb takes the scroll, an outburst of worship erupts in heaven. The four living creatures (angelic beings/cherubim) and the twenty-four elders (often understood as representing the redeemed) fall prostrate before the Lamb in a posture of worship. This is highly significant: it affirms that the Lamb (Jesus) is fully divine, for only God is to be worshiped. Indeed, heavenly beings would never worship anyone less than God 28. The fact that the whole heavenly court falls down before Jesus is a powerful testimony to His deity and worthiness.

Each of these worshipers is holding two things: harps and golden bowls of incense. These items are rich with symbolism drawn from Old Testament worship. Harps were instruments used in the Temple and by the psalmists to accompany songs of praise to God (see Psalm 33:2, 98:5) 29. Here, in heavenly worship, the harps indicate the praise and joy of the redeemed. In Revelation, harps are associated with worshipful music in heaven (Revelation 14:2, 15:2). Thus, we see that music and rejoicing are part of the worship of the Lamb - an expression of delight in His victory.

The golden bowls full of incense are said to be "the prayers of the saints." In the Old Testament Tabernacle/Temple, priests would burn incense on the altar of incense, and its fragrant smoke would rise before God (Exodus 30:7-8). This was a symbol of prayers ascending to God's presence, a connection explicitly made in Psalm 141:2 ("Let my prayer be set before You as incense") and later in Revelation 8:3-4 30. Incense in golden bowls suggests precious, divinely-sanctioned prayers being offered to God. The imagery here shows that the worshiping elders present the prayers of God's people to Him like sweet incense. In other words, the cries and petitions of believers (often for justice, deliverance, God's kingdom to come) are not forgotten; they are a fragrant offering before God's throne.

Some dispensational commentators note that these "prayers of the saints" likely refer to the prayers of believers who are awaiting deliverance on earth - especially the persecuted tribulation saints (possibly the faithful remnant of Israel) during the end times 31. At the very moment when heaven is about to unleash judgment, the accumulated prayers for vindication and redemption as often echoed in the Psalms - are presented to God. This assures us that our prayers play a role in God's plan. God in His sovereignty has chosen to work in concert with the prayers of His people. The bowl imagery shows the prayers are heard and stored up until the proper time when He will answer them in full.

In application, verse 8 encourages us that our prayers matter in heaven. Every true prayer - whether for personal need or for God's justice - is like incense before the Lord. At the right time, He will respond. Also, note the beautiful combination in worship: joyful praise (harps) and intercession (incense) go hand in hand. As we worship Christ, we rejoice in His worthiness and also bring our pleas to Him. True worship involves reverence, music from the heart, and the lifting of prayers. How wonderful that even in glory, the redeemed are depicted as both worshipers and priests - praising God with harp-like joy and also serving in a priestly role by bringing prayers to God 15. Indeed, this foreshadows our destiny as "a kingdom of priests" to God (v.10). Our future is one of intimate worship and holy service. Even now, we should live out that calling - delighting in Christ and interceding in prayer, confident that our worship rises to the very throne of God.

Revelation 5:9-10 - The New Song of Redemption

"And they sung a new song, saying, 'Thou art worthy to take the book, and to open the seals thereof: for Thou wast slain, and hast redeemed to God by Thy blood out of every kindred, and tongue, and people, and nation; and hast made them unto our God a kingdom of priests, and they shall reign on the earth'" (5:9-10). The worshiping elders and living creatures now burst into song - a new song directed to the Lamb. In Scripture, a "new song" often signifies praise for a new act of salvation or a fresh revelation of God's work (Psalm 98:1, "sing to the Lord a new song, for He has done marvelous things"). Here, the newness likely points to the unprecedented work of redemption accomplished by Christ 32. As one writer explains, it's not "new" in time (there have been songs before), but new in kind - "something previously unknown and unprecedented" 32. Indeed, the salvation wrought by the Lamb God the Son becoming man and dying to ransom people from sin - is the marvelous new work that inspires this unique song in heaven.

Let's break down the lyrics of this heavenly hymn, as it is loaded with doctrine and hope. The song highlights five key truths about Christ's redemptive achievement 33 34:

  1. The Lamb's Worthiness Is Rooted in His Sacrifice: "Worthy are You to take the scroll and to open its seals, because You were slain..." The song gives the reason for Jesus' unique worthiness to execute God's plan: His atoning death 33. From heaven's perspective, the crucifixion far from being a defeat - is the very ground of Christ's exaltation (cf. Philippians 2:8-9). By letting Himself be slain for sinners, Jesus fulfilled God's righteous demands and thus is worthy to usher in judgment and the kingdom. This teaches the doctrine of Christ's merit: He alone, by virtue of the cross, deserves to rule and redeem. Application: We, too, should never lose the wonder that the all-powerful Lion gained His right to rule by becoming a slain Lamb. It's the paradox at the heart of the gospel and should fill us with awe and gratitude.
  2. Redemption Accomplished by His Blood: "...and You have redeemed to God by Your blood..." The word "redeemed" here can also be translated "purchased" 35. It evokes the image of a slave market Christ's blood was the purchase price to set captives free (1 Peter 1:18-19). Jesus Himself said He came to give His life "as a ransom for many" (Mark 10:45). Thus, the song celebrates that Jesus' death ransomed people for God. Notably, it says "redeemed to God" meaning the purpose of redemption is to bring people into relationship with God, for His possession and glory. This is a deeply personal and relational view of salvation: by His blood, Jesus wins us for God Himself. Worthiness, then, is not based on our performance, but entirely on Christ's "personal blood sacrifice," which made God's grace available to us 36 37. Application: This moves us to personal worship - "Jesus paid it all; all to Him I owe." We have been bought with a price, so we belong to God (1 Corinthians 6:20).
  3. A People from Every Tribe and Language: "...out of every kindred, and tongue, and people, and nation." The scope of Christ's redemption is universal in its reach. He did not die for one ethnic group or one nation only, but for people from every group. In Genesis 12:3, God promised Abraham that "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed." Here we see the fulfillment - Jesus' work gathers a redeemed people from all tribes, languages, peoples, and nations. This fourfold description emphasizes the beautiful diversity of Christ's Church. In the throne room later (Revelation 7:9) John sees the great multitude "from every nation, tribe, people, and tongue" worshiping the Lamb. Christ's blood breaks down all racial, cultural, and linguistic barriers. Heaven will be the most multi-ethnic, multilingual assembly imaginable, all united by the Lamb's blood. The song thus praises Jesus as the Savior of the whole world, not just a local deity. Application: Do we share heaven's passion for the global reach of the gospel? Christ gave His life to save people very different from us - this should inspire us toward missions, evangelism, and love for all our brethren in Christ, no matter their background. It's also a comfort: God knows and seeks His worshipers in every obscure corner of the earth. No one is too far or too insignificant to be redeemed.
  4. Made a Kingdom of Priests: "And You have made them (or us, in some manuscripts) a kingdom and priests to our God..." This echoes Exodus 19:6 where God said Israel would be "a kingdom of priests" and 1 Peter 2:9 calling believers "a royal priesthood." Because of Jesus' redemption, those He saves are not just freed from sin, but elevated to a royal and priestly status 34. As kings, we are destined to share in Christ's rule (Romans 8:17 says we are co-heirs with Christ). As priests, we have the privilege of direct access to God and the calling of worship and intercession. The Plymouth Brethren, in particular, often emphasize the heavenly calling of the Church that even now, spiritually, we are seated with Christ in the heavenlies and have priestly access to God. William Newell wrote, "Those in Christ HAVE their redemption... have been made meet to be partakers of the inheritance... They are not of the world... have been made to sit with Him in the heavenlies" 38 39. Our priestly service now is to offer spiritual sacrifices (praise, prayer, self-giving) to God (Hebrews 13:15). In the future, it appears we will have roles of service in God's kingdom administration as well. This point in the song shows the dignity of the redeemed - Christ not only saves us from something (sin and wrath), but saves us to something glorious (a kingdom and priesthood). Application: It's humbling and inspiring to realize our identity in Christ. We should live as royal priests even now not in pride, but in holiness and devoted service to God. Do I approach God freely in prayer as a priest? Do I exercise the authority of Christ's truth and love in this world as part of His royal embassy?
  5. Destined to Reign on Earth: "and they shall reign on the earth." This looks forward to the future Millennial Kingdom and beyond, when Christ returns and establishes His rule on Earth (Revelation 20:4-6). Amazingly, the redeemed are said to reign with Him. This fulfills promises like 2 Timothy 2:12, "If we endure, we shall also reign with Him," and Revelation 3:21, "To him who overcomes I will grant to sit with Me on My throne." While Christ is King of kings, He graciously shares His reign with His people they are the "kings" and "priests" serving under His supreme authority. Brethren expositors often stress that this reign will be literal and earthly, in the 1000-year kingdom age where Jesus restores Israel and rules the world from Jerusalem, and the Church in glorified bodies reigns at His side 40. Thus, the ending of the song points to our hope: not only have we been saved and brought near to God, but our destiny is to govern and steward the renewed earth with Christ. No wonder the elders sing with such enthusiasm! This is the ultimate reversal of the Fall - humans, once slaves to sin, are now through Christ made rulers over a redeemed creation, fulfilling God's original intent (Genesis 1:26, Psalm 8:4-6). Application: Knowing our future encourages perseverance now. We shall reign on the earth - therefore, we can suffer loss or persecution in this present time with the confidence that a far greater honor is coming (Romans 8:18). It also motivates us to live responsibly; as future co-regents with Christ, we should cultivate faithfulness and wisdom now (Luke 19:17).

All together, this "new song" is a rich theological anthem celebrating Christ's worthiness and the results of His redeeming work. It's doctrinal (mentioning blood, redemption, kingdom, etc.) and doxological (directed in worship to Christ). Imagine the emotion and reverence of this moment: those who sing are themselves beneficiaries of the Lamb's sacrifice - they sing not abstractly but from personal experience of salvation. It's been well said that only the redeemed can truly sing of redemption's joy. Angels can join in the praise (as we see next), but only humans can sing of being ransomed by blood. This is likely why the 24 elders (symbolizing redeemed humanity) take the lead in this song 15.

For our personal spiritual life, we can join in this song even now. Every time we worship Jesus for His cross, we are echoing this heavenly hymn. Do I daily remember that He is worthy? Do I marvel that I am redeemed by His blood and called to be His priest and heir? Such reflection should stir us to fresh worship - indeed, to a new song in our hearts. When worship grows cold, Revelation 5:9-10 can rekindle the fire: "Worthy are You... for You were slain, and have redeemed us to God!" Let this be the theme of our lives and future.

Revelation 5:11-12 - Myriads of Angels Praise the Lamb

"And I beheld, and I heard the voice of many angels round about the throne and the living creatures and the elders: and the number of them was ten thousand times ten thousand, and thousands of thousands; saying with a loud voice, 'Worthy is the Lamb that was slain to receive power, and riches, and wisdom, and strength, and honor, and glory, and blessing." (5:11-12). As the elders and living creatures sing the new song of redemption, suddenly the angelic hosts join in a resounding chorus. John looks and now becomes aware of an uncountable multitude of angels encircling the throne, adding their voices. Their number is described as "myriads of myriads and thousands of thousands" 41. In essence, this means innumerable an echo of Daniel 7:10 ("thousands of thousands ministered to Him; ten thousand times ten thousand stood before Him"). It's as if all the angels in heaven cannot even be counted, and all are now involved in praising the Lamb.

Interestingly, angels were not explicitly mentioned in chapter 4's throne-room scene; they appear here in force 26. This reinforces the earlier point that at the moment the Lamb takes the scroll, angels assume a distinct supporting role, while the focus centers on the Lamb and the redeemed. In chapter 4, the living creatures gave praise without mention of angels; but now the angels clearly voice their adoration too 26. They stand around, forming a grand outer circle around the throne and elders. If one can imagine the scene: at the center is God's throne and the Lamb, immediately around the throne are the four living creatures, then the 24 elders on their thrones (Revelation 4:4), and now beyond them, filling the expanse of heaven, millions upon millions of angelic beings. All are united in a single purpose: to exalt Jesus, the Lamb of God.

The angels cry out in a loud voice a doxology of sevenfold praise: "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain to receive power and riches and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and blessing." This seven-term doxology attributes to Christ every form of excellence and honor. It's not that Jesus only now "receives" these things (He is inherently possessor of them), but rather it's the angels ascribing and extolling these attributes and the right of Christ to claim their full exercise. Let's briefly consider these seven terms of praise:

  • Power - All might and authority belong to Jesus. He is omnipotent and sovereign.
  • Riches - He is entitled to all wealth and resources of creation. (This could mean spiritual riches or the honor of all offerings given to Him.)
  • Wisdom - Christ has all wisdom and deserves to be recognized as the source of truth and guidance.
  • Strength - Similar to power, emphasizing His sheer might or capability to act.
  • Honor - The reverence and esteem of all beings are due to Him.
  • Glory - All glory (splendor, fame) is His by right; He is the radiance of God's glory.
  • Blessing - All praise and good spoken words (literally eulogy) should be directed to Him.

In short, every possible commendation and value is heaped upon Jesus. The number seven indicates completeness - the angels are ascribing total perfection to the Lamb in His worthiness 42. Notably, this kind of doxology parallels the worship of God the Father. In Revelation 4:11, God was praised as worthy to receive glory, honor, and power for creation. Here Jesus is praised in even fuller terms for redemption. This once again shows that Jesus is worshiped equally with the Father, a testament to His divine status 43. The Lamb shares in the very glory of God.

It's worth noting that the angels do not mention "You redeemed us" - they cannot sing the new song of personal redemption (Christ did not die for angels, and faithful angels never needed redemption). Instead, the angels' focus is on the worthiness of the Lamb and His attributes. They are essentially admiring the Lamb for what He has done and who He is. 1 Peter 1:12 says angels desire to look into the things of our salvation, and here we see them marveling and rejoicing in it. They are thrilled at God's plan and Christ's triumph, even though it was not for themselves. What a picture of selfless worship!

For our application, consider the explosive growth of praise here: it started with a few (living creatures and elders), and then multiplied to innumerable voices. Praise is infectious the more we gaze on Christ, the more hearts (even angelic ones) are drawn into the worship. We're also reminded that all creation in heaven is totally Christ-centered. If angels, who never sinned, are so exuberant in extolling the Lamb who was slain, how much more should we, who are the very beneficiaries of that slaying, overflow with praise! Also, the comprehensiveness of the doxology challenges us to avoid half-hearted worship. Jesus deserves all we have all honor, all glory. Do our prayers and songs reflect even a fraction of this heavenly enthusiasm? We can start now to echo the angels' cry: "Worthy is the Lamb!" - letting that shape our daily attitude of adoration towards Jesus.

Revelation 5:13 - Every Creature Joins in Praise

"And every creature which is in heaven, and on the earth, and under the earth, and such as are in the sea, and all that are in them, heard I saying: 'Blessing, and honor, and glory, and power, be unto Him that sitteth upon the throne, and unto the Lamb forever and ever." (5:13). As if the chorus of angels were not enough, now all of creation is pictured as raising its voice in praise. John hears every creature - whether in heaven (all heavenly beings), on earth (all living people and perhaps animals), under the earth (the dead and possibly demonic spirits in Hades), and in the sea (all sea creatures) - everything in the entire universe acknowledges the glory of God and the Lamb. This scene echoes and fulfills verses like Philippians 2:10-11, "at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord" 44. It also recalls Psalm 69:34, "Let heaven and earth praise Him, the seas and everything that moves in them." In essence, universal worship is given to God and the Lamb.

The praise in verse 13 is directed to "Him who sits on the throne" (God the Father) and "the Lamb" (God the Son). This is critical: the same blessing, honor, glory, and power are ascribed to both Father and Son together, forever and ever. Here we have one of the clearest portrayals of the Son sharing equal worship with the Father. The combined worship of the Father and Jesus "proves that Christ is God" as the commentary noted 43. No creature could join the Creator in receiving worship; for the Lamb to be included shows His co-equal deity. We behold a Trinitarian scene of worship (the Spirit is present as the sevenfold Spirit in verse 6, and the worship is to the Father and Son). For all eternity, the Triune God will be the object of all praise.

It's interesting that even the realm of the lost ("under the earth") is included. This indicates that ultimately even God's enemies will have to acknowledge His supremacy - not in a saving sense, but in a compelled recognition of reality. The praise of "every creature" likely includes even those who are not redeemed. They will bow and confess Christ's lordship (Phil. 2:11) for some, in joy, for others, in regret and defeat. Every knee will bow. As one author noted, "Every creature, including the demonic world and unsaved humanity, will ultimately praise Christ as Lord" (even if that acknowledgment comes at the final judgment) 44. This shows how absolutely total Christ's victory is. No voice will be able to dissent when He is revealed in glory.

The fourfold doxology here "blessing, honor, glory, power" parallels the earlier praises, and again emphasizes the completeness and perfection of worship that is due. "Forever and ever" (to the ages of ages) stresses that this worship is eternal. There will never come a time when Christ ceases to be adored.

For us, verse 13 is a grand reminder of our chief end: to glorify God and enjoy Him forever. It also encourages us that one day the discord of this world will be resolved into perfect praise. Right now, not every creature praises God - far from it. Many blaspheme or ignore Him. But the day is coming when the entire universe will be in unison, acknowledging the greatness of our God and Savior. This gives us hope and perspective. When we feel discouraged by the God-defying spirit of the age, we can remember the preview of this verse. It's also a missionary mandate: God deserves the worship of every creature. As the missions slogan says, "Let the nations be glad!" - we evangelize so that more and more voices will gladly join this choir before the forced acknowledgment comes. Ultimately, God will see to it that He and the Lamb are glorified by all creation. Hallelujah!

Revelation 5:14 - "Amen!" Heavenly Worship Continues

"And the four living creatures said, 'Amen'. And the twenty-four elders fell down and worshipped Him that liveth forever and ever" (5:14). This final verse in the chapter shows the concluding response of the inner circle of worshipers. The four living creatures (the cherubim) punctuate the universal praise with "Amen," a solemn affirmation meaning "so be it" or "truly" 45. It's like they are sealing the praise with divine approval Yes, truly, all blessing and honor belong to God and the Lamb. The living creatures have been guardians and proclaimers of God's holiness (cf. Rev 4:8); here they endorse the glory given to God and the Lamb with their Amen.

Meanwhile, the elders once again prostrate themselves in worship. They had already fallen before the Lamb in verse 8; now they fall down in worship of God ("Him that liveth forever and ever"). Some manuscripts explicitly include worship of the Lamb here too (some add "and worshiped Him that liveth forever and ever" implying both Father and Son) 46, but regardless, the picture is of utter adoration and submission by the redeemed before God's eternal majesty. The elders' posture - face down, casting crowns as in Rev 4:10 perhaps - shows that even though we are made kings and priests, our highest joy and duty is to worship our God forever.

And with that, the scene in heaven closes. Chapter 6 will move into the action of opening the seals. But chapter 5 ends on the note of worship. This teaches us that worship is the ultimate response to God's revelation. Before any judgment falls on the wicked earth, heaven is first filled with praise. All theology (knowledge of God) should lead to doxology (praise of God). Here we've learned of the Lamb's worthiness, and the immediate result was an outpouring of worship from all beings. If studying Revelation 5 does not lead our hearts to say "Amen!" and bow in worship, we have missed the main point.

In personal application, we can take verse 14 as a model. When we pray "Amen" in our prayers, let it be with the same heartfelt agreement to God's glory that these living creatures had. And let us cultivate the habit of bowing our hearts in awe regularly. In our personal devotions, we can even literally kneel or fall on our face in prayer not as a ritual, but as a sincere expression of God's worth in our lives. The elders falling down "forever and ever" indicates that worship is never ending; it won't be a duty but our eternal delight. The Westminster Catechism famously says man's chief end is "to glorify God and enjoy Him forever." Revelation 5 shows that in action: glorifying God is the enjoyment of heaven.

Conclusion:

Revelation chapter 5 gives us a glorious glimpse into heaven's perspective on Christ's redemptive work. We saw that God's final plan (the scroll) is centered on Jesus Christ, the only worthy Redeemer. He alone, as the Lion of Judah and Lamb of God, conquered sin and death through His sacrifice 19. Because of this, He is worthy to unleash God's judgments and bring in the Kingdom. Heaven exults in who Jesus is and what He has done: the Lamb slain, who by His blood purchased a people from every nation and will make them reign as kings and priests 47 34. The entire cosmos responds in worship first the redeemed and angels, and eventually "every creature" acknowledges that Jesus and the Father are worthy of all praise 44. This chapter beautifully ties together doctrine and devotion: it teaches profound truths about Christ's person and work (doctrine), and it shows the fitting response of adoring worship and hope (devotion).

For a personal Bible study, Revelation 5 is immensely enriching. Doctrinally, it strengthens our understanding of Christ's deity (since He is worshiped like God 43), His atonement (redeeming us by His blood), and God's sovereign plan for history (the sealed scroll of destiny). It also supports the premillennial hope that the redeemed will reign with Christ on earth in the future Kingdom 48. Plymouth Brethren expositors particularly love this passage for its clear distinction of the Church in heaven (the elders) before tribulation, and the rightful heir (Christ) taking His authority. Devotionally, Revelation 5 beckons us to respond as those elders did: with harp in hand and bowl of incense, joyfully praising and praying in the presence of Jesus 15. It encourages us that our prayers are precious to God 30, and that Jesus cares about the culmination of God's plan - so much that He died to achieve it and now lives to open it. We are invited to "behold the Lamb" daily to see our Savior in His humility and glory, and to say, "Worthy is the Lamb!"

In your personal walk, when you face sorrow or feel the world's chaos, remember John's tears and the elder's words: "Weep not... behold, the Lion... has prevailed" 19. Jesus is worthy and He is victorious. Nothing and no one else in all creation can fix our plight - but He can and will. So we cling to Him. Let the vision of Revelation 5 elevate your worship. Perhaps spend time meditating on each phrase of the new song (verses 9-10), turning it into personal praise: "Lord Jesus, You are worthy... You were slain for me... You redeemed me to God... You have made me Yours... To You be glory forever!" As you do, you join the very worship of heaven. This is the ultimate "personal application" - aligning our hearts with the chorus of eternity.

Finally, Revelation 5 assures us that history is not random; it is scroll-written and Christ-held. The Lamb who shed His blood for us holds the future. We can trust Him with the big picture of the end times and with the details of our lives. He will unfold God's plan perfectly, in justice and mercy. The chapter that follows (Revelation 6) will show tribulations on earth, but we have already seen the throne room of heaven a throne of grace for us now, and a throne of righteous judgment to come. Knowing this, we live in peace and expectation. Even so, come Lord Jesus! And until He does: "Blessing and honor and glory and power be to Him... and to the Lamb forever!" Amen. 44 49
