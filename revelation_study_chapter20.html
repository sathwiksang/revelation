Revelation Chapter 20

In-Depth Theological and Personal Bible Study (NKJV)

Context and Overview

Revelation 20 is a pivotal chapter in Christian eschatology, describing the Millennial Kingdom (the thousand-year reign of Christ) and the final judgment of evil before the eternal state. In Revelation 19, Jesus Christ returned in glory to defeat the beast (Antichrist) and the false prophet and their armies. Now Revelation 20 reveals what happens after Christ's Second Coming: Satan is bound, the saints reign with Christ for a thousand years, Satan's final rebellion is crushed, and the wicked are judged at the Great White Throne. This chapter is crucial doctrinally (for understanding the "Millennium," the resurrections, and final judgment) and devotionally (encouraging believers with the ultimate triumph of God's kingdom and warning of the finality of judgment). We will study verse by verse (NKJV), with cross-references and insights from Plymouth Brethren theologians and similar premillennial authors, drawing both doctrinal understanding and personal application.

Revelation 20:1-3 - Satan Bound for a Thousand Years

"Then I saw an angel coming down from heaven, having the key to the bottomless pit and a great chain in his hand. He laid hold of the dragon, that serpent of old, who is the Devil and Satan, and bound him for a thousand years; and he cast him into the bottomless pit, and shut him up, and set a seal on him, so that he should deceive the nations no more till the thousand years were finished. But after these things he must be released for a little while." (Revelation 20:1-3, NKJV)

John's vision opens with an angel from heaven seizing Satan (the "dragon" or "serpent of old" cf. Revelation 12:9) and locking him in the "bottomless pit" (Greek: abussos, the abyss) for 1,000 years. This "binding" of Satan is literal and future, not merely symbolic of Christ's victory at the cross, because Scripture shows Satan still actively deceiving the world in the present age (cf. 1 Peter 5:8). Plymouth Brethren expositors note that during this Millennium, Satan will no longer be able to "prowl about the world ensnaring and destroying," as he is now 1. The abyss is sealed shut, temporarily incapacitating Satan's influence over the earth's nations 2. Importantly, this is not Satan's final judgment (that comes later in verse 10); it is a major restriction of his activity during Christ's earthly reign 1. Jesus Himself hinted at this future confinement when demons begged Him not to send them "into the abyss" before the appointed time (Luke 8:31), indicating that a set time for such demonic imprisonment was known 3. Immense will be the relief for humanity and creation when the evil deceiver is removed from the scene 3. Without Satan's corrupting influence, the world will experience righteousness and peace unparalleled in history.

During these thousand years, deception will cease ("that he should deceive the nations no more") – implying a vast change in the world's spiritual environment. This fulfills numerous prophecies of an age when truth and the knowledge of God fill the earth (e.g. Isaiah 11:9 "the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD"; Habakkuk 2:14). It does not mean every person will be inwardly converted (as we will see later); rather, outward temptation and satanic lies are removed from society 4. Righteousness will be enforced by Christ's government, and open evil quickly punished (see Isaiah 11:4, Psalm 2:9) 4. The Millennium is therefore characterized by external peace and justice, though not yet the absolute sinlessness of the eternal state 4.

For believers today, this promise of Satan's binding offers great hope and motivation. We currently wrestle with spiritual warfare against the devil's schemes (Ephesians 6:11-12), but we know God has ultimate power over Satan. At the appointed time, even a single angel (by God's authority) can bind Satan with ease – a reminder that the devil is not God's equal, but a created being under God's sovereignty. This encourages us to persevere in resisting Satan now (James 4:7), knowing his power is limited and his doom is sure (Revelation 12:12). It also comforts us that all the injustices and sorrows Satan has caused will be addressed in God's plan.

Finally, note the ominous hint: "after these things he must be released for a little while." God, in His wisdom, has ordained a final brief release of Satan after the Millennium. The word "must" shows this is part of God's purpose – as we will discuss in verses 7-10, this final test will reveal the true state of human hearts even after a thousand years of ideal conditions. In application, this teaches us that apart from God's grace changing the heart, even a perfect environment does not guarantee loyalty to God. The problem of sin lies in human hearts, not merely in our environment. This sobering truth should drive us to seek the new birth and heart transformation now (John 3:5-7), rather than thinking improved circumstances alone can produce righteousness.

Revelation 20:4-6 - The First Resurrection and Christ's Millennial Reign

"And I saw thrones, and they sat on them, and judgment was committed to them. Then I saw the souls of those who had been beheaded for their witness to Jesus and for the word of God, who had not orshiped the beast or his image, and had not received his mark on their foreheads or on their hands. And they lived and reigned with Christ for a thousand years. But the rest of the dead did not live again until the thousand years were finished. This is the first resurrection. Blessed and holy is he who has part in the first resurrection. Over such the second death has no power, but they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with Him a thousand years." (Revelation 20:4-6, NKJV)

After describing Satan's binding, John's vision shifts to the glory of the saints reigning with Christ. He sees "thrones, and they sat on them, and judgment was given to them." Who are these enthroned ones? Scripture teaches that the saints will share in Christ's rule and judgment (e.g. Daniel 7:22 "judgment was given to the saints," 1 Corinthians 6:2 "the saints will judge the world," and Revelation 5:10 "we shall reign on the earth"). Plymouth Brethren commentators identify these throne-sitters as the redeemed people of God who were already glorified and in heaven at Christ's return 5. Earlier in Revelation, the 24 elders, the Bride of the Lamb, and the heavenly armies following Christ all symbolized the Church and Old Testament saints in glory 5. Now, in the Millennium, these glorified saints accompany Christ and share His governing authority over the earth: "There are thrones filled with persons come from heaven in the train of Christ, who now reign along with Him" 5. In other words, all believers who were caught up with Christ (at the Rapture/resurrection prior to or at His Second Coming) are already resurrected and enthroned to reign with Him in the Millennium 7. Jesus promised to overcomers, "To him who overcomes I will grant to sit with Me on My throne" (Revelation 3:21), and here we see that promise fulfilled as they reign under the King of kings.

John then sees another group: "the souls of those who had been beheaded for their witness to Jesus... and who had not worshiped the beast." These are the martyrs of the Great Tribulation – faithful believers who were executed for Christ during the end-times persecutions (Revelation 6:9; 13:7, 15) 8 9. By specifically mentioning those who refused to worship the beast or receive his mark, the text identifies two groups of Tribulation saints: (1) those earlier in the tribulation who were slain "for the word of God" (as seen under the altar in Rev. 6:9-11) 10, and (2) those later during the beast's tyrannical rule who refused his mark and image 9. John initially sees them as "souls" - indicating they were in a disembodied state due to martyrdom 11. But then he says, "they lived and reigned with Christ for a thousand years." The phrase "they lived" means they were brought to life – in other words, resurrected. This resurrection of the Tribulation martyrs happens at the start of the Millennium, allowing them to join the already enthroned saints in Christ's kingdom 12 13. William Kelly explains that John first saw these faithful ones in their martyred state, "and next states that 'they lived.' It is a raising up of both classes from the dead to join others already raised... Thus only were they enabled to reign with Christ." 12 13 God vindicates these sufferers; though Antichrist killed them, God raises them to glory, true to the promise that "If we endure, we shall also reign with Him" (2 Timothy 2:12).

John is careful to note that "the rest of the dead did not live again until the thousand years were finished." These "rest of the dead" are all the unsaved who were not part of the first resurrection. They will not be raised to life until after the Millennial reign (they appear in verse 12 at the Great White Throne). This makes it plain that there are two separate resurrections, one for the righteous and one later for the wicked, with a thousand-year gap between 14. The apostle John elsewhere spoke of a "resurrection of life" and a "resurrection of condemnation" (John 5:29). Here in Revelation 20 these two are separated in time. As J.N. Darby observed, "The apostle speaks of the first resurrection, as if all knew that there are two resurrections. In the Word of God, two resurrections are always spoken of, and never one general resurrection...God does not confound the just and the unjust" 15. Scripture consistently teaches this distinction (see Luke 14:14 where Jesus mentions "the resurrection of the just," implying a different event from the resurrection of the unjust). Thus, "this is the first resurrection" (verse 5b) is a concluding statement to John's vision of the thrones and the martyred saints. It encompasses all the righteous who will be raised to reign with Christ - including Christ Himself (the "firstfruits" - 1 Corinthians 15:23), the Church saints (raised at the Rapture), Old Testament saints (cf. Daniel 12:2) and these final Tribulation saints 16. All these are "the first resurrection" class, distinguished from the resurrection of "the rest of the dead" (the unbelievers) which comes later 14. In summary, the first resurrection is the resurrection to eternal life and glory for believers, whereas the second resurrection (implied later) is a resurrection to judgment for the lost. "Blessed and holy is he who has part in the first resurrection. Over such the second death has no power," but they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with Him a thousand years. The "second death" is defined in verse 14 as the lake of fire (eternal damnation). If you belong to Christ and share in the first resurrection, hell has no claim on you – your eternity is secure with God. This beatitude echoes Jesus' promise in John 5:24: those who believe in Him "have passed from death into life", and will not come into condemnation. What an assurance for the believer!

Furthermore, those in the first resurrection "shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with Him a thousand years." This combines two roles: priesthood and kingship, which Christ's redeemed people are repeatedly said to possess (see Revelation 1:6, 5:10, 1 Peter 2:9). In the coming Kingdom, glorified believers will serve God as priests - enjoying direct fellowship with Him and likely interceding on behalf of mankind - and rule with Christ as kings - carrying out administrative authority under the supreme King. This was God's design from the beginning (man was to have dominion under God, Genesis 1:26), now realized under the perfect Man Jesus Christ, the Last Adam. Believers reigning with Christ fulfills the promise that "if we endure, we shall also reign with Him" and that the overcomers will have "power over the nations" to "rule them with a rod of iron" alongside Christ (2 Timothy 2:12, Revelation 2:26-27). Darby notes that Christ's personal reign on earth is essential - "A reign of Christ without Christ is a reign without a King...It must be the man Jesus manifested in glory" 17 18. Thus He will personally assume world throne as King of Kings (Matthew 25:31, Zechariah 14:9) and we who belong to Him will be at His side.

The Millennial Kingdom here described - 1000 years of Christ's righteous rule on earth with His saints - is a major theme of biblical prophecy. Though the term "millennium" (from Latin mille thousand) isn't explicitly in Scripture, Revelation 20 uses "a thousand years" six times, and this period corresponds to the Kingdom Age foretold by the Old Testament prophets 19 20. The prophets saw a coming era of Messiah's reign when the world would be filled with peace, justice, and the knowledge of God. For example:

  • Isaiah described a time when "a King will reign in righteousness" (Isa. 32:1) and the nature of creation itself will be transformed: "The wolf also shall dwell with the lamb... the lion shall eat straw like the ox... They shall not hurt nor destroy in all My holy mountain, for the earth shall be full of the knowledge of the LORD" (Isaiah 11:6-9). He foresaw Israel restored to God, dwelling safely "under their own vine and fig tree" in peace 21 22, and even the desert blooming (Isa. 35:1) and the infirm healed (Isa. 35:5-6). He called this ruler "Wonderful, Counselor, Mighty God, Prince of Peace," whose government and peace increase without end on the throne of David (Isaiah 9:6-7). All these await fulfillment in this thousand-year reign of Christ on earth.
  • Jeremiah likewise prophesied the restoration of Israel and Judah under "the LORD our Righteousness," a descendant of David, when "Judah will be saved and Israel will dwell safely" (Jeremiah 23:5-6) 23. He foresaw a new covenant where "they shall all know Me" (Jer. 31:34; cf. Heb. 8:11) 24.
  • Ezekiel added details about Israel's resurrection as a nation (the valley of dry bones in Ezek. 37), a new temple and worship system (Ezek. 40-48), and God's glory returning to dwell among His people, with the city renamed "The LORD is There" (Ezekiel 48:35) 25 26.
  • Daniel saw a "Son of Man" receive an everlasting kingdom from the Ancient of Days, displacing all previous empires (Daniel 7:13-14, 7:27) 27. He interpreted the stone that smote the image and grew into a great mountain filling the earth as the coming kingdom of God (Dan. 2:34-35, 44) 28.
  • Micah echoed Isaiah about nations beating swords into plowshares and not learning war, when "the LORD reigns from Zion" (Micah 4:1-3) 29 30. Zechariah foretold the Messiah reigning as King in Jerusalem, all nations coming yearly to worship the King and celebrate the Feast of Tabernacles (Zech. 14:9, 16-17), and even the streets of Jerusalem filled with playing children in safety (Zechariah 8:4-5) 31. Malachi spoke of the Lord coming suddenly to His temple to purify and rule, and the sun of righteousness rising with healing (Mal. 3:1-3; 4:1-2) 32.

In Revelation 20, we finally see the culmination of all these prophecies. It is, as Ephesians 1:10 says, "the dispensation of the fullness of the times" when God gathers together all things in Christ 33. The Kingdom of God visibly arrives on earth as Christ and His saints administer the world in righteousness. "Righteousness will reign" during this Millennium (in contrast to today, when righteousness suffers, and the eternal state, when righteousness "dwells" permanently see 2 Peter 3:13) 34. It will be a time of unparalleled blessing, yet as we will see, not everyone born in that age will submit sincerely to Christ in their heart.

Personal Application (Reigning with Christ): The truth of the first resurrection and the coming Kingdom should deeply encourage us. Believers can look forward to a very personal share in Christ's victory - we are not only saved from sin, but called to "glory and honor and immortality" in His kingdom (Romans 2:7). Knowing this destiny, we are motivated to live a holy life now ("everyone who has this hope in Him purifies himself", 1 John 3:3). We should embrace our identity even now as a "royal priesthood" (1 Peter 2:9), drawing near to God in prayer and representing Christ to the world. Also, suffering for Christ now is put in perspective by the promise of reigning with Him then (Romans 8:17-18). If you are undergoing trials or persecution, remember that "if we suffer, we shall also reign with Him" – He will not forget our sacrifices. Additionally, this doctrine challenges us to be mission-minded: Only those who belong to Jesus will have part in the first resurrection. We should earnestly desire that others come to saving faith now, so they too can be blessed and avoid the "second death." It also gives us hope for this world's future – no matter how unjust or chaotic things are now, Jesus will set it right in His kingdom. As believers, we pray "Thy Kingdom come" (Matthew 6:10) with greater joy and understanding, knowing the incredible peace and glory that Kingdom will bring.

Revelation 20:7-10 - Satan's Final Rebellion and Defeat

"Now when the thousand years have expired, Satan will be released from his prison and will go out to deceive the nations which are in the four corners of the earth, Gog and Magog, to gather them together to battle, whose number is as the sand of the sea. They went up on the breadth of the earth and surrounded the camp of the saints and the beloved city. And fire came down from God out of heaven and devoured them. The devil, who deceived them, was cast into the lake of fire and brimstone where the beast and the false prophet are. And they will be tormented day and night forever and ever." (Revelation 20:7-10, NKJV)

After a thousand years of peace and righteousness, Satan is released from the abyss "for a little while" (as God forewarned in verse 3). Immediately, he resumes his old work of deception, targeting the nations across the whole earth ("four corners" implies the entire globe). Despite a millennium of Christ's benevolent rule, there will be a vast number of people - "as the sand of the sea" - who fall prey to Satan's lies and follow him into rebellion against God. This shocking development is rich with theological significance. It demonstrates the sad truth that even the perfect environment of Christ's kingdom will not regenerate the human heart. As one Brethren writer notes, "It shows us the incorrigible evil of the heart of man if left to himself... While Satan is shut up... many [will only] feign obedience... When the devil is loosed... he finds a host...ready to do his bidding" 35 36. John N. Darby similarly remarks, "Jesus manifested in glory does not change the heart. This change is a work of grace. As soon as those...who have seen the glory are no more kept from temptation by the power of God... they fall" 37. In other words, outward conformity during the Millennium is not the same as inward transformation. During the thousand years, people will still need to personally trust in Christ for salvation. Many will, but apparently many will not – complying outwardly under Christ's rod of iron, yet inwardly resentful or unbelieving. When Satan is released to tempt, he exposes these unconverted multitudes. This final rebellion proves definitively that man's heart is desperately wicked apart from God's grace (Jeremiah 17:9), and that only those truly born again will remain loyal to God. It forever silences any argument that a utopian environment alone can perfect humanity - even after experiencing utopia, some will choose evil when given the chance.

The deceived rebels are gathered by Satan for one last attempt to overthrow "the camp of the saints and the beloved city." The "beloved city" is clearly Jerusalem, the capital of Christ's kingdom on earth (Isaiah 2:2-4, Zechariah 14:16-17). The "camp of the saints" suggests the people of God in general dwelling securely around Jerusalem, perhaps an innumerable assembly of righteous from all nations who have come to worship. The names "Gog and Magog" are used, echoing the prophecy of Ezekiel 38-39 where Gog of Magog invades Israel. Here, the terms are likely symbolic, indicating the enemies of God from all parts of the world (like saying "from the four corners, Gog and Magog" to encompass all hostile forces). This is probably not the same event as Ezekiel's prophecy (which many see as earlier, possibly around the tribulation period); rather, John borrows the names to describe a similar type of invasion – a vast horde coming against God's people 38. In any case, the emphasis is on the scale of this rebellion (massive, global) and its target (Christ's government centered in Jerusalem). One might wonder how so many could rebel after experiencing Christ's reign. William Kelly points out that during the Millennium the earth's population will explode to numbers "far exceeding" anything in history 39 – lifespans will be longer (Isaiah 65:20-22 suggests people living the entire era), and with war and famine removed, humanity will multiply greatly 39 40. So by the end of 1000 years, there could be billions of people. Sadly, "secret elements of evil" will have been kept under control during Christ's reign, but not eliminated from hearts 4 41. Thus a multitude "as the sand of the sea" rallies to Satan.

The outcome of this final battle is swift and decisive – no prolonged war is described. As the rebels surround God's people, "fire came down from God out of heaven and devoured them." This echoes how God has, in the past, rained fire on blasphemous rebels (e.g. the judgment on Sodom and Gomorrah in Genesis 19:24, or on Gog's forces in Ezekiel 38:22). Here it indicates a direct divine intervention that instantly destroys the attacking armies. Satan's plan is utterly crushed. This illustrates that God's power cannot be overthrown - ever. Even after a thousand years of Satan stewing in the abyss, he emerges only to meet immediate defeat by God's fiery judgment. For believers, this is a comforting truth: no matter how frightening the forces of evil may gather, God's protection of His people is sure and His victory is certain. The saints did not even have to fight; God Himself delivers them.

Finally comes the ultimate fate of the evil one: "The devil, who deceived them, was cast into the lake of fire and brimstone where the beast and the false prophet are. And they will be tormented day and night forever and ever." Satan is thrown into the Lake of Fire, a place of everlasting punishment originally "prepared for the devil and his angels" (Matthew 25:41). There he joins the beast and false prophet, who, notably, have been there since the start of the Millennium (see Revelation 19:20). After a thousand years, the beast and false prophet are still in the lake of fire – refuting any idea of annihilation. The text says "where the beast and false prophet are", indicating their conscious existence in torment even after centuries 42. This underscores the frightening truth that the Lake of Fire is eternal punishment - "day and night forever and ever" - with no rest or release. The devil, the instigator of so much evil from Eden onward, meets his final doom here, never to afflict God's creation again. For the universe to be completely cleansed, the chief antagonist is now removed forever. Evil will never rise again.

Personal Application (Warning and Hope): The final rebellion and Satan's end teach us a few practical lessons. First, it's a warning against mere outward religion. It is possible to "conform" externally to Christianity (even in a future golden age) yet not be truly saved. Ultimately, a heart unchanged by grace will fall away. "You must be born again" (John 3:7) - there is no substitute. We should examine ourselves that our faith is genuine, not simply a product of our environment or upbringing. Second, we learn about God's justice and patience. God gives even that final generation a choice (allowing Satan's release to test them) - He never forces anyone to love Him. Tragically many choose evil, and God brings swift judgment. This should motivate us now to choose Christ sincerely and share the gospel, so hearts can be changed before judgment comes. Third, we find hope and comfort: All the forces of darkness will inevitably be vanquished by God. When you feel overwhelmed by evil in the world, remember that in the end, fire from heaven will consume the enemy. God's people are ultimately safe in His hands; "no weapon formed against you shall prosper" (Isaiah 54:17) when you stand with the Lord. And finally, Satan's destiny reminds us that hell is real and awful – if the devil and his angels, and later all whose names aren't in the Book of Life, are consigned there, we should soberly consider the eternal stakes. Yet rejoice that through Jesus Christ, we are delivered from that fate (1 Thessalonians 1:10) and will witness the day when the deceiver is gone forever. What a relief and joy that will be for creation - no more tempter, no more spiritual war! As believers, this inspires a heartfelt "Hallelujah!"

Revelation 20:11-15 - The Great White Throne Judgment

"Then I saw a great white throne and Him who sat on it, from whose face the earth and the heaven fled away. And there was found no place for them. And I saw the dead, small and great, standing before God, and books were opened. And another book was opened, which is the Book of Life. And the dead were judged according to their works, by the things which were written in the books. The sea gave up the dead who were in it, and Death and Hades delivered up the dead who were in them. And they were judged, each one according to his works. Then Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death. And anyone not found written in the Book of Life was cast into the lake of fire." (Revelation 20:11-15, NKJV)

In this final section of Chapter 20, John's vision shifts to a somber courtroom scene: the judgment of all unsaved humanity before the Great White Throne of God. This occurs after the Millennium and after the final rebellion is quashed. The description "earth and heaven fled away from His face" indicates that the present universe is dissolved or passes away at this point 43. Indeed, 2 Peter 3:10-12 speaks of the heavens and earth being burned up and removed. No place is found for them - they vanish, making way for a coming "new heaven and new earth" (Revelation 21:1). This tells us that the Great White Throne judgment transpires outside of our present space-time world - it is a transcendent event, possibly in God's heavenly court or an entirely new dimension, since the old creation is gone. The throne is described as "great" (signifying its ultimate authority) and "white" (signifying perfect holiness and justice). The One seated on it is God Himself – specifically, God the Son, Jesus Christ, to whom all judgment is committed (John 5:22, "The Father has committed all judgment to the Son"). This is not a judgment believers should fear if we belong to Christ, because Scripture is clear that those who trusted in Jesus have their sins forgiven and will not be condemned in this judgment (John 5:24, Romans 8:1). In fact, the "first resurrection" saints (all believers) have already been raised and rewarded by this point. The focus here is on "the dead" - explained in verse 5 as the rest of the dead who hadn't been raised yet, i.e. the unbelieving dead from all ages.

John sees these dead, "small and great," standing before God's throne. No one is exempt - from the most obscure person to the greatest emperors, all who died apart from Christ now face their Creator. The imagery of books being opened indicates that God has a complete record of every person's deeds (compare Daniel 7:9-10, which also depicts thrones and books opened in a final judgment scene). One book in particular is highlighted: the Book of Life. This book contains the names of all who belong to the Lord – those saved by His grace. We've encountered it before in Revelation (e.g. Rev. 3:5, 13:8). At the Great White Throne, the Book of Life is opened as evidence in this judgment 44. Yet the passage implies that none of the dead standing there have their name in it – it is opened to demonstrate that fact. "Anyone not found written in the Book of Life was cast into the lake of fire" (v.15). As Kelly notes, "Why then is the Book of Life mentioned? Not because any of [the judged] names were written therein, but in proof that they were not. The book of life will confirm what is gathered from the [record] books." 44 45 In other words, the absence of their names in the Life book seals their fate, showing they never received God's gift of life. They rejected the salvation offered through Christ, and thus their names are not recorded among the redeemed.

Meanwhile, the other books contain a thorough account of each person's works – every thought, word, and deed (cf. Ecclesiastes 12:14, Luke 8:17). God's judgment is totally just: these dead are "judged according to their works, by the things which were written in the books." This does not mean they could be saved by good works - in fact, since none have their name in the Book of Life (indicating they lacked saving faith), their works can only condemn them. The righteous standard is God's perfection, and their deeds will inevitably fall short (Romans 3:23). But the degree of punishment can be understood as commensurate with their deeds – other scriptures imply some will have a heavier judgment than others (e.g. Luke 12:47-48 speaks of differing stripes, Matthew 11:21-24 of it being "more tolerable" for some than others in judgment). Thus, the record books ensure absolute fairness – no one will be able to argue with God's verdict, because the evidence of their own lives, down to every secret, will agree with His judgment. God's justice will be vindicated. As one commentator puts it, "There was the sad register of undeniable sins on the one side; [and] no [name] on the other side. Thus...all conspire to declare the justice...of God's final irrevocable sentence." 45 46

Verse 13 emphasizes the universal scope of this resurrection and judgment: "The sea gave up the dead who were in it, and Death and Hades delivered up the dead who were in them. And they were judged, each one according to his works." No matter where people died - even lost at sea - God will resurrect them. "Death and Hades" together is a way of saying the state of death (the body in the grave and the soul in Hades, the realm of the dead) releases all its captives. Every last soul is brought forth to face judgment. This refutes the idea that the unsaved simply cease to exist or remain in oblivion; all will be raised to face God. Each person is judged "according to his works" - repeated for emphasis. It bears repeating: nobody at this judgment is saved by works; rather their works are the evidence used for their condemnation, since their sins are not washed by Jesus' blood. Believers' sins were judged at the cross (Isaiah 53:5-6), and believers have appeared at the Judgment Seat of Christ (2 Corinthians 5:10) earlier to be evaluated for rewards - but not for salvation, since that was settled by faith. In fact, Scripture teaches that true believers will not face this Great White Throne condemnation: Jesus said, "He who hears My word and believes in Him who sent Me has everlasting life, and shall not come into judgment, but has passed from death into life" (John 5:24, NKJV). Romans 8:1 assures "There is therefore now no condemnation to those who are in Christ Jesus." Thus, what we witness at the Great White Throne is the judgment of the lost. (One possible exception: some scholars ponder whether people born during the Millennium who trusted Christ and perhaps died during that time might be raised here but found written in the Book of Life, thus spared. However, it's likely that very few if any millennial believers die - Isaiah 65:20 implies longevity, and some suggest God will translate millennial saints into the eternal state without death 47 48. In any case, Revelation does not explicitly show any saved being judged here. The plain statement of verse 15 is that only those not in the Book of Life are cast into fire, implying any in the book would not be cast in.)

Verse 14 adds that "Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire." Death and Hades are treated as entities or forces that are now abolished. This signifies the ultimate defeat of death itself. Death (the last enemy, 1 Corinthians 15:26) is thrown into hell, so to speak - meaning no more death will ever occur for the rest of eternity. Hades (the realm of departed spirits) is no longer needed, because after this judgment there will be no more waiting place of the dead; everyone's eternal fate is sealed, and for the redeemed there is direct dwelling with God (Revelation 21:3-4). "This is the second death." The Bible defines "second death" here as the Lake of Fire - an eternal spiritual death (separation from God) following physical death. The first death is physical; the second death is the eternal punishment of body and soul in hell (Matthew 10:28). There is a solemn finality in that phrase. The chapter ends with the stark pronouncement that anyone not found in the Book of Life is cast into the lake of fire. It's a dreadful prospect: an eternity in the lake of fire, conscious and unending ("day and night forever" as verse 10 said). This is the ultimate "wages of sin" (Romans 6:23). It demonstrates God's holiness and justice - He has given every opportunity for repentance (throughout history, the witness of conscience, His revelations, the sacrifice of Christ, the Millennial kingdom itself), but those who persist in rejecting Him face this righteous judgment. There is no hint of escape or appeal at this point. The verdict is final. And thus history, as we know it, reaches an end.

Personal Application (Live in Light of Eternity): The scene of the Great White Throne should instill in us a profound sobriety and urgency. For one, it calls us to thankfulness and worship - if you are saved, it is solely by God's grace that your name is in the Book of Life. Rejoice that Jesus took the judgment you deserved, and your future is secure in Him! "Blessed are those who are written in the Lamb's Book of Life" (cf. Luke 10:20, Philippians 4:3). This gratitude should lead us to evangelism and intercession. Knowing that anyone not in the Book of Life will face this terror, we should earnestly desire and pray for the salvation of others - our loved ones, neighbors, and the unreached. There is a real, eternal hell to avoid, and a real, eternal heaven to gain. God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked (Ezekiel 33:11); neither should we. Let's be faithful to point people to Christ, "who delivers us from the wrath to come" (1 Thess. 1:10).

For our personal holiness, this reminds us that what we do in life truly matters. Believers won't be condemned for sin at the Great White Throne, but we will give account of our lives (Romans 14:10-12). And for the unbeliever, every deed is remembered. There is no "getting away" with evil. If not repented of and covered by Christ's blood, every sin will be called to account by a just God. This truth can help us when we struggle with forgiveness or see injustice prospering - we can release vengeance to God, knowing He will repay justly (Romans 12:19). No unrepentant crime or cruelty will go unanswered. On the flip side, if you have not received Christ yet, take this warning seriously: you do not want to face God's throne carrying the record of your own works. No one measured up to His perfect standard. Flee to Jesus now; have your name written in His Book of Life by trusting in Him. As Scripture urges, "It is appointed for men to die once, but after this the judgment" (Hebrews 9:27). There's no second chance after death – so make sure you settle your case by accepting the free salvation Jesus offers, before you stand before the Judge.

Lastly, the casting of Death into the fire means death will die. For believers, this is a triumphant hope: "Death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Corinthians 15:54). We will see the day when death, our last enemy, is forever banished and "God will wipe away every tear" (Revelation 21:4). So even as we tremble at the Great White Throne's seriousness, we also rejoice that beyond it lies a future with no more dying, no more pain for God's children. The end of Revelation 20 sets the stage for Revelation 21-22, where a new heaven and new earth emerge and God dwells with His redeemed people eternally.

Conclusion: Theology and Personal Response

Revelation 20 is a foundational passage for Christian eschatology, especially for those of premillennial conviction (like the Plymouth Brethren and similar evangelical teachers). It clearly teaches a future literal 1000-year reign of Christ on earth with His saints 20, vindicating God's promises and highlighting His righteousness. It reinforces the doctrine of two resurrections – one of the just (to glory) and one of the unjust (to judgment) 15. It reveals the ultimate defeat of Satan and the absolute justice of God in judging sin. As William Kelly wrote, in this chapter we see that after these events, humanity is "either in the resurrection state for good or for ill; and thus it must be forever" 49. There is an air of finality and destiny in Revelation 20 that should profoundly impact us.

Doctrinally, it calls us to align with God's plan: to ensure we partake in the first resurrection by being united to Christ, and to uphold the truth of Christ's coming Kingdom against allegorical interpretations that deny its reality 50. Theologically, it showcases God's sovereignty (He controls even Satan's ultimate fate), His faithfulness (fulfilling covenants to Israel and the saints in the Millennial blessings), His grace (raising martyrs to reign), and His holiness (not clearing the guilty at the final judgment).

For our personal spiritual life, Revelation 20 offers both comfort and challenge. We are comforted that evil will not reign forever - one day, every wrong will be made right. We draw comfort that we will live and reign with our Savior, experiencing the joy of His victory firsthand. We also feel the challenge to live now in light of these future realities: to pursue holiness fitting for a future priest and king, to endure trials with the hope of glory, and to urgently witness to others out of love, so that they too might avoid the second death. This chapter ultimately directs our eyes to Jesus Christ - the King of kings and Judge of all. It is He who binds Satan, He who raises the dead, He who reigns in righteousness, and He who sits on the throne. Our destiny hinges on our relationship to Him. For the believer, these verses stir awe and hope – "Thanks be to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ!" (1 Cor. 15:57). For the unbeliever, these verses warn to flee to Christ.

As you meditate on Revelation 20, allow both its doctrinal richness and personal implications to sink in. Rejoice in the promised Kingdom, take sin and salvation seriously in light of the coming judgment, and let the vision of Satan's defeat and death's destruction fill you with hope. We can echo the early Christians' prayer: "Even so, come, Lord Jesus!" - looking forward to the day when He will rule openly and bring in everlasting righteousness.
