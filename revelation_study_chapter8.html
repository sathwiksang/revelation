Revelation Chapter 8

The Seventh Seal and the First Four Trumpets

Background and Context

Revelation chapters 7 and 8 form a pivotal section of the book, blending God's mercy and judgment during the end times. Chapter 7 is a parenthesis or interlude between the sixth and seventh seals of judgment [1]. In this pause, God reveals His saving work: the sealing of 144,000 from Israel and the vision of a great saved multitude from all nations. Chapter 8 then resumes the narrative with the opening of the seventh seal, which leads into the seven trumpet judgments.

From a Plymouth Brethren (dispensational) theological perspective, the Church has been raptured by this point, represented symbolically by the 24 elders in heaven (see Rev. 4-5). The events of Revelation 6-18 occur during Daniel's 70th week (a future Tribulation period). In this framework, God is dealing with Israel (fulfilling covenant promises to a remnant) and with the unbelieving world, while also saving a multitude of Gentiles out of the Tribulation. This study will proceed verse by verse through Revelation 7 and 8, integrating doctrinal insights and personal applications, with parallel Scriptures and commentary from Plymouth Brethren authors.

Revelation Chapter 8 - The Seventh Seal and the First Four Trumpets

Chapter 8 brings us back to the chronological progression of judgments. The seventh seal is opened, and it dramatically leads into a new series of judgments symbolized by seven trumpets. The structure to note is: the seven seals were like the overview of end-time woes, and the 7th seal contains the seven trumpet judgments (and later, the 7th trumpet will contain the seven bowl judgments). So there's a telescoping effect. Here, we will see the first four trumpets, which are often grouped together as judgments largely affecting the natural world (earth, sea, rivers, heavens). These are sometimes called ${}^{4}1/3$ judgments" because they repeatedly affect one-third of various things. They are punitive but partial - a warning of worse to come in the three Woes (trumpets 5, 6, 7).

Revelation 8:1 - Silence in Heaven at the Opening of the Seventh Seal

"And when He opened the seventh seal, there was silence in heaven for about half an hour" (8:1). The Lamb (Jesus) breaks the final seal of the scroll. Instead of an immediate catastrophe, John is struck by a profound silence in heaven lasting "about half an hour." Considering earlier scenes were full of worship songs and loud proclamations, this silence is arresting. It's as if all of heaven holds its breath. The opening of the final seal is so solemn that it demands silence a dramatic pause before the storm. One Brethren commentator calls it "a brief pause of solemn expectancy, the lull that precedes the storm"[22]. The prayers and praise cease momentarily, creating an atmosphere of awe. It reminds us of verses like Habakkuk 2:20, "But the LORD is in His holy temple. Let all the earth keep silence before Him." Also Zephaniah 1:7, "Be silent in the presence of the Lord GOD; for the day of the LORD is at hand." Heaven's silence indicates the gravity of what God is about to do as the trumpet judgments commence.

Some older interpreters fancifully suggested this half-hour silence represents the calm of the Millennium or a break in prophetic fulfillment. But William Kelly refutes that as "wholly imaginative and without one solid reason" [23]. The more natural understanding is one of awe and preparation. In the Old Testament temple liturgy, there were moments of silence when the priest went in with incense (Luke 1:9-10 the people were praying silently outside as Zechariah offered incense). This links to the next verses where we see an angel with a censer. Thus, the silence could also be the collective pause as the prayers of the saints ascend (8:3-4).

Personal Application:

Silence can be a form of worship and preparation. In our noisy world, do we ever fall silent before God? This heavenly silence teaches us reverence. Sometimes words fail and we must be still, acknowledging God's holiness and power. When we anticipate God's action or seek His guidance, a period of silent waiting can show our trust and respect (Psalm 46:10). Also, the half hour (a finite time) suggests that God's judgments, though fearful, are measured and deliberate - He is never in haste or chaos; everything is perfectly timed. We too can trust God's timing in our lives, learning patience in the pauses.

Revelation 8:2-5 - The Heavenly Altar: Prayers Rise and Fire Falls

John then sees a scene in heaven's temple: "And I saw the seven angels who stand before God, and to them were given seven trumpets" (8:2). These are a special cadre of angels, possibly archangels, who stand before God (perhaps akin to Gabriel, who said "I am Gabriel, who stands in the presence of God," Luke 1:19). They are handed seven trumpets, ready to sound in sequence. Trumpets in biblical times were used for signaling important events calls to war, assembly, or announcing kings (Numbers 10:1-10, Joel 2:1). Here, each trumpet blast will signal a new judgment from God on earth. But before they start blowing, another action takes place:

"Then another angel, having a golden censer, came and stood at the altar" (8:3). This is reminiscent of the Old Testament priest who would take a censer of coals from the bronze altar and then offer incense on the golden altar before the veil (Leviticus 16:12-13). In the heavenly temple that John sees (which the earthly tabernacle foreshadowed), there is an altar of incense before God's throne. The angel is given "much incense, that he should offer it with the prayers of all the saints upon the golden altar which was before the throne" (8:3). As he offers it, "the smoke of the incense, with the prayers of the saints, ascended before God from the angel's hand" (8:4).

This is a powerful symbolic picture of intercession. The prayers of believers (likely the cries for justice, deliverance, and God's kingdom to come including the martyrs' plea "How long, O Lord" from Rev. 6:10) are viewed as rising in the aromatic smoke of incense up to God. Incense in Scripture often represents prayer (Psalm 141:2, "Let my prayer be set before You as incense"). Here we see that God has not ignored the prayers cried out during the Tribulation; He has collected them, so to speak, and now as they reach the full measure, He is about to answer.

Many expositors (especially in the Brethren tradition) see this "another angel" as a representation of Christ Himself in a priestly role [24]. The language "another angel" might make us think it's just a created angel among others, but consider: in the Old Testament, the Son of God often appeared as the "Angel of the Lord," performing divine acts (e.g. Judges 6:21, Zech. 3:1-6). Here, the function of this angel is distinctly priestly - offering incense with the prayers of saints - which parallels Jesus' unique role as our High Priest who adds the sweet incense of His merit to sanctify our prayers (Hebrews 7:25, Romans 8:34). William Kelly notes that under the trumpets, "the Lord Jesus assumes an angelic character", appearing not now as the Lamb opening seals, but as the Angel of the Covenant in priestly garb [24] [25]. He hastens to add this doesn't mean Jesus isn't incarnate; it's an aspect of the vision language. This interpretation underscores that our prayers are effective because of Jesus' intercession. If it is Christ, we see Him here as Mediator, presenting our prayers to God with the fragrant incense of His own perfection. If it is not Christ but a high-ranking angel, the scene still teaches that God receives the prayers of the saints formally in heaven's sanctuary they are a priority before He acts further.

Now comes the answer to those prayers in dramatic form: "Then the angel took the censer, filled it with fire from the altar, and threw it to the earth" (8:5). Immediately there are "noises, thunderings, lightnings, and an earthquake." This imagery is striking - the very censer that carried the prayers upward now hurls fire downward. It signifies that the prayers for God's judgment against evil and vindication of the righteous are being answered by fire from the altar of heaven itself. Fire from the altar, which normally consumed sacrifices, is now flung earthward as a tool of judgment. This reminds us of Ezekiel 10:2, where in a vision an angel took coals of fire from between God's throne cherubim and scattered them over Jerusalem a symbol of God's wrath on the city. In Revelation 4:5, God's throne was accompanied by lightning, thunder, etc., indicating His majesty and impending judgment. Here, similar phenomena erupt as the prelude to the trumpets. God has heard, and now God will act. As Darby puts it, "The intercession [of Christ] turned into judgments in the answer" [26] - meaning the time for pleading is done; the time for avenging has come, in response to those very pleas.

Observe that the sequence of silence incense/prayer fire cast down teaches a cause-and-effect: the prayers of the saints are the catalyst for the coming judgments. This is profound. God, in His sovereignty, has chosen to involve the prayers of His people in executing His plan. The martyrs under the altar were told to rest "a little while" (Rev. 6:11) - now their blood will be avenged as part of the trumpet woes. The church for ages has prayed "Thy kingdom come" now the steps toward the Kingdom's arrival (through tribulation judgments) are taking place. Thus, prayer is not wasted; it's stored in golden bowls (Rev. 5:8) and here in golden censers, until the divine response comes at the proper time.

After the fire is thrown, the stage is set: the seven trumpet-angels prepare to sound (8:6). The connection is clear: judgments fall because of and in answer to the prayers. This should both encourage and sober us about prayer. Encourage, because it shows every prayer according to God's will is noted in heaven - even if the answer seems delayed, it will come in power. Sober, because sometimes the answer to prayers like "Thy will be done" involves judgment that is devastating. Indeed, the saints were praying for God's justice; when justice comes, it's a day of woe for the world.

Personal Application:

Incense and fire from the altar paint a picture of two aspects of Christ's work: intercession and judgment. As believers, we take comfort that Jesus intercedes for us our prayers are mixed with the incense of His perfect prayer and presented acceptably to God (Heb. 4:15-16, our prayers ascend through our merciful High Priest). This should embolden us to pray more and with greater fervor, knowing our prayers reach God's throne. Are we taking advantage of this privilege? Notice it says "prayers of all the saints" - not just leaders or martyrs, but all believers' prayers count. Even the feeblest prayer, if sincere in Christ's name, rises as incense before the Almighty.

On the other hand, this scene reminds us that persistent prayer for God's kingdom and righteousness implies we are also praying for the end of this present world's system. When we pray for Jesus to return, we are in effect praying for the judgment of the wicked (because His coming involves both salvation and judgment). This should give us urgency to also pray for the lost to repent before it's too late. It also reassures us in times when evil seems rampant: God hasn't ignored injustice; He's storing up the cries and, when the censer's tipped, His judgment will be thorough. As Romans 12:19 says, "Vengeance is Mine, I will repay, says the Lord."

Finally, consider the altar. For the believer, the altar of Christ's cross was where judgment fire fell on our Substitute, and from that altar we now have the fire of the Holy Spirit and prayer. But for those who reject Christ, the altar fire becomes the fire of wrath. Ensure your life is on the right side of the altar trusting in the Lamb who was slain so that the fire purifies you now (trials, discipline) and your prayers are sweet incense, rather than facing the unmitigated fire of judgment later.

Revelation 8:6-7 - The First Trumpet: Hail, Fire, and Blood upon the Earth

With the heavenly prelude completed, the first angel now sounds his trumpet (8:7). Immediately, "hail and fire, mingled with blood" are thrown to the earth. A third of the earth is burned up, a third of the trees are burned up, and all green grass is burned up. This judgment targets the land (earth) and vegetation.

This plague has echoes of the plagues on Egypt. Exodus 9:23-25 describes the seventh plague: Moses stretched his rod to heaven and the Lord sent thunder, hail, and fire (lightning) running along the ground, which struck down all vegetation and killed any people or animals exposed. The difference here is the inclusion of blood with the hail and fire. Possibly, blood could signify lightning or volcanic meteors causing bloody slaughter, or a phenomenon where water in hail turns red (some speculate atmospheric coloring). More likely it signals that this storm is extremely lethal - not just damaging property, but causing bloodshed. The result is a fiery devastation: one-third of the earth's surface (perhaps one-third of land area) is scorched. One-third of trees (think of fruit trees, forests long-term resources) are burned, and all the grass (at least in the affected regions) is burned, meaning immediate destruction of food for livestock and of natural beauty.

Many interpreters take this as a literal catastrophe a mix of hail and fire (like a terrible thunderstorm with fiery elements, possibly meteoric fragments) that results in widespread wildfires. The inclusion of "blood" might indicate this storm causes much death, so blood is splattered, or it could be a supernatural red rain. Either way, this is an ecological disaster of huge proportions. Some have even speculated it could describe the fallout of a nuclear exchange or massive volcanic eruptions (which can send fiery debris and blood- like rain). However, nothing in the text requires a "modern" explanation; as with Egypt's plagues, God can supernaturally send such a plague.

Plymouth Brethren scholars often see symbolic meaning as well: In prophecy, the "earth" can symbolize organized society or a particular region. As mentioned, "the third part" is likely the sphere of the old Roman Empire (Europe/Mediterranean world) [27]. Thus, the first trumpet could imply a divine judgment on the stable civilized world (the "earth") in that region - perhaps political upheaval or war that consumes leaders ("trees" often symbolize great men see Judges 9:8-15) and ordinary people ("grass" may symbolize common humanity[28]). William Kelly interprets this trumpet as a "violent down-pouring of [God's] displeasure" on the earth, where hail a storm of judgment from above, fire God's consuming wrath, and blood = death [29]. He suggests the life in view may be spiritual life - i.e. that this judgment signifies a spiritual death or apostasy overtaking many, rather than physical death alone [30]. Kelly also notes the "third" recurring pattern is best understood as the Roman earth (western world) [27]. In that light, the burning of a third of trees and all grass could indicate the collapse of a third of worldly authorities and the withering of general prosperity in the affected society [28]. Indeed, he says "dignitaries within that sphere were consumed, [and] there was also a universal interference with the prosperity of men"[28].

It's possible both the literal and the symbolic are true a literal environmental judgment that also topples societal structures. The key idea is a targeted, but severe judgment from God on the earth, resulting in significant destruction. And yet it's limited to one- third - which implies God is warning, not utterly destroying (not yet).

Parallel Scriptures: This trumpet brings to mind Joel 2:30-31, where God says "I will show wonders in heaven and in the earth: blood and fire and pillars of smoke." Also Isaiah 2:12- 13 speaks of God's day coming upon all the cedars of Lebanon (tall trees) - symbolically high people. And Isaiah 24:6 says in a global judgment "therefore the inhabitants of the earth are burned, and few men are left." These could be allusions to the same end-time conflagrations.

Personal Application:

The first trumpet judgment shows how quickly the things we rely on food sources, green fields, stable climates can be touched by God. As much as humanity prides itself on controlling nature, a single divine command can wither the earth. This should humble us to live lightly with material things. It echoes James 1:10-11, that the rich man fades away in his pursuits just like grass withers under the burning heat. We should put our trust not in the security of creation, but in the Creator. Also, consider the judgment aspect: this rain of fire and hail is reminiscent of Sodom's judgment (Genesis 19:24). God is a consuming fire (Hebrews 12:29). For those under Christ's blood, His fire refines - for those in rebellion, it devours.

Additionally, the partial nature (one-third) is a mercy - God is saying "I could justly destroy all, but I restrain My anger to a third, giving opportunity to repent." Tragically, Revelation 9:20 indicates that many will not repent even after these trumpets. But the call for us now is: repent while there is time! Jesus used similar language when He spoke of disasters: "Unless you repent, you will all likewise perish" (Luke 13:3). Environmental devastation in Revelation also reminds us not to idolize nature; rather, seeing creation's destruction should point us to the Creator's righteousness and the urgency to be right with Him.

Revelation 8:8-9- The Second Trumpet: A Burning Mountain into the Sea

The second angel sounds, and John sees "something like a great mountain burning with fire was thrown into the sea" (8:8). The result: a third of the sea became blood, a third of the living creatures in the sea died, and a third of the ships were destroyed (8:8-9).

John says "something like" a great burning mountain he may be struggling to describe exactly what he sees. A mountain ablaze plunging into the ocean certainly calls to mind a volcanic eruption (volcanoes are essentially burning mountains) or perhaps a giant meteor or asteroid impacting the sea (from John's perspective it'd look like a fiery mass as big as a mountain). The effect described is threefold: sea turned to blood, massive death of sea life, and destruction of ships. This parallels the first Egyptian plague (waters to blood, killing fish Exodus 7:20-21). A literal fulfillment could be an asteroid impact causing widespread oceanic catastrophe: the force could kill marine life over a vast area and generate megatsunamis that wreck ships in a third of the ocean. If one imagines, for example, an asteroid hitting the Atlantic one-third of that ocean turning to blood (dead organisms) and tsunamis wiping out ships and coastal areas.

Another angle: In biblical symbolism, a "mountain" often represents a kingdom or empire (see Daniel 2:35, Jeremiah 51:25). Jeremiah 51:25 actually calls Babylon a "destroying mountain" that God will make a "burnt mountain." Interestingly, in that same chapter Babylon's destruction is linked with the sea (Jer. 51:42, "The sea has come up over Babylon; she is covered with its waves"). Thus, some see this trumpet as figurative: a great political power or nation, enflamed with revolution or judgment, is hurled into the turbulent "sea" of nations [31]. The sea often symbolizes the Gentile nations (Daniel 7:3, Revelation 17:15). If so, the prophecy might indicate a major empire's downfall that causes horrific bloodshed and disruption through the world (especially affecting trade, as ships are destroyed). Kelly interprets the "burning mountain" as "a great earthly power" under God's judgment crashing into the unruly masses (the sea), resulting in their destruction and halting of commerce (ships) [31]. This could be depicting the fall of "Babylon the Great" or some key center of power during the tribulation, an event elaborated in Rev. 17-18. In fact, Revelation 18:17-19 laments the destruction of Babylon and that "every shipmaster" cries over the ruin of their trade, which aligns with ships being destroyed here.

Whether literal or figurative or both, the second trumpet devastates the oceans and maritime affairs by one-third. Turning the sea to "blood" means either actual blood from countless dead creatures or a blood-red toxic sea (could be algal blooms or chemical changes). The death of marine life and destruction of ships would cripple food supplies (fish) and global commerce (as so much trade is by sea). Perhaps John specifically noting "ships" hints at the economic impact it's not just nature suffering, but human industry too.

Parallel Scriptures: Besides Jeremiah 51, another link is to Exodus 7:20 (Nile turned to blood). Also, Isaiah 2:16 speaks of the Lord's day coming on "all the ships of Tarshish," i.e., humbling maritime commerce. And Ezekiel 27 portrays the fall of Tyre (a trading power) with ships lamenting - a foreshadow of end-time commercial collapse.

Personal Application:

The burning mountain reminds us that no kingdom or institution of man is secure if it sets itself against God. In one prophetic moment, the proud "mountain" of human power is cast down. "The Lord of hosts will have a day against all that is proud and lofty" (Isaiah 2:12). If we apply this personally: Jesus said if we have faith we could say to a mountain "be removed and cast into the sea" (Mark 11:23) - mountains can represent insurmountable problems or proud obstacles. God can indeed obliterate the "mountains" in our path or in society in His timing. On the flip side, let us ensure our security isn't in any earthly mountain, whether a nation, economy, or institution, because God will shake all things (Heb. 12:27). Our trust should be in "the Rock of Ages," not in the mountains of this world.

Also, this judgment affecting the sea and ships could speak to material loss. Many in John's day and ours make their living from the sea (trade, fishing). The sudden loss of a third of ships and livelihoods warns us not to be idolaters of wealth. Colossians 3:5 equates covetousness with idolatry. The fall of commercial Babylon in Revelation is a judgment on greed and luxury. So, the trumpet cautions: hold wealth loosely, it can disappear overnight. Instead, store treasure in heaven where no disaster can touch it (Matthew 6:19-20).

Lastly, consider God's just retribution: The wicked are often described as the "sea" (restless, casting up mire, Isaiah 57:20). A burning mountain being cast on the sea can symbolize the fire of judgment meeting wicked humanity. It's reminiscent of Jesus saying "Whoever causes one of My little ones to stumble, it'd be better if a millstone were cast into the sea" a graphic fate (Luke 17:2). Here a fiery mass is cast into the sea very apocalyptic. It should create in us a healthy fear of God's power and a longing to be counted among the faithful, not among those upon whom such woes fall.

Revelation 8:10-11-The Third Trumpet: The Great Star "Wormwood"

Now the third angel sounds his trumpet. John sees a brilliant object: "a great star fell from heaven, burning like a torch" (8:10). This meteor-like star falls on a third of the rivers and springs (the fresh waters). The star has a name: Wormwood. As it poisons the waters, they become bitter, and "many men died from the water, because it was made bitter" (8:11).

Wormwood is a very bitter herb (artemisia absinthium) known in biblical times. In Scripture it symbolizes bitterness, sorrow, and judgment. For example, Proverbs 5:4 compares immorality to wormwood; Jeremiah 9:15 speaks of God punishing Israel by feeding them "wormwood and giving them water of gall to drink;" likewise, Jeremiah 23:15 uses wormwood to describe God's judgment on false prophets. So wormwood is associated with bitter affliction as a consequence of sin.

In this trumpet, a "star" (Greek aster) falls from heaven. This could be an actual celestial body (comet or meteor) that breaks apart and contaminates a significant portion of earth's freshwater sources with some toxin (perhaps chemical or radioactive). One could imagine the breakup of a comet's pieces falling into various watersheds, making rivers poisonous. The fact that it specifically says "rivers and springs" (the sources of drinking water) means this trumpet strikes at humanity's water supply. If one-third of fresh water becomes undrinkable, you can foresee famine, disease, and death ("many men died"). The name Wormwood given suggests God's purposeful action He names it as the agent of bitterness.

Alternatively (or additionally), there may be a symbolic meaning: Stars can represent angels or prominent figures. For instance, in Rev. 9:1 a star falling from heaven is clearly an angel (who is given a key to the abyss). In Isaiah 14:12, Lucifer is called the "morning star" fallen from heaven. If Wormwood were an angelic or demonic being, it could mean a demonic power influences the world to make life bitter. More likely in context, many Brethren writers see the star as a metaphor for a leader/teacher who falls and brings harm. J.N. Darby writes that the star signifies "one that should have been a special source of light and order... [who] fell from his place, and corrupted the moral sources... They became bitter, and men died of $it^{\prime\prime}$[32]. In other words, perhaps a great leader (political or religious) turns apostate, and his influence poisons the minds and hearts of people (the "waters" often symbolize teachings or influences that people "drink"). This interpretation sees "many men died from the waters" as spiritual death due to poisonous doctrine or unrighteous laws disseminated by this fallen star. Similarly, Kelly says it is "the fall of a great dignitary or ruler, whose influence was judicially turned to poison all the springs and channels of popular influence" [33] corrupting society's moral life and causing many to perish. Historically, some have likened this to figures like Attila the Hun or a future False Prophet type figure.

Perhaps both the literal and figurative hold: a literal meteor could hit waters even as a symbolic "fall" of a star-like figure occurs. Interestingly, the Chernobyl nuclear disaster of 1986 introduced a footnote in prophecy discussions because "chernobyl" is Ukrainian for wormwood. After that reactor explosion, local water was contaminated and there were bitter consequences, which some saw as a small foreshadow. Whether or not Revelation 8:10-11 points to something nuclear, it certainly conveys poison and death entering mankind's water.

Water is essential for life; turning it bitter is a direct strike on human sustenance. In contrast, recall that when Israel entered the wilderness and found bitter waters at Marah, Moses (by God's power) turned them sweet (Exodus 15:23-25). Here, God in judgment turns sweet waters bitter - the opposite of the Exodus grace. It's as though God says, "You have rejected the Living Water (Christ); now taste the bitterness of sin's result."

Parallel Scriptures: Besides the wormwood verses in Jeremiah, Amos 5:7 says, "You who turn justice to wormwood, and cast down righteousness to the earth" - implying societal injustice makes life bitter for many. That fits the moral interpretation (corrupt leaders turning justice bitter). Also, a prophetic parallel: in the $1/3$ judgments, perhaps Zechariah 13:8 can be a background, which mentions $2/3$ destroyed, $1/3$ left refined - though that's more for Israel's refinement.

Personal Application:

On a personal level, "wormwood" represents the bitter fruits of sin. Hebrews 12:15 warns of any "root of bitterness" that can spring up and defile many possibly alluding to Deut. 29:18 where anyone who turns from God is called a "root bearing bitter poison." We must be vigilant that no wormwood of false teaching or bitterness of spirit contaminates the "fresh water" of our faith and fellowship. One person's sin or false doctrine can poison a whole community if not checked (Galatians 5:9).

This trumpet also underscores human dependence on God's provision. Do we give thanks for clean water? Often we take it for granted. Lamentations 3:19 uses wormwood as a metaphor for grief, but then says, "Great is Your faithfulness." Every refreshing drink ultimately comes from God's faithful supply. Should He touch it in judgment, we'd quickly feel our fragility. Thus, cultivate gratitude for basics like water, food, air - and don't pollute these gifts physically or spiritually.

Additionally, leadership matters. If the star is a leader, his fall shows how a public figure's collapse (through pride or wickedness) can negatively impact many lives. We must not put our hope in any human "stars," but only in Christ. And if we are in leadership positions (as parents, teachers, pastors), we must walk circumspectly, lest our stumble bring bitterness to those under our influence. It's sobering that the name of this star is remembered as Wormwood - a legacy of bitter poison. How will we be remembered? As sources of life or bitterness? By God's grace, may we be like stars that lead to living water (pointing people to Jesus), not falling stars that bring ruin.

Revelation 8:12 - The Fourth Trumpet: Darkness on Sun, Moon, and Stars

When the fourth trumpet is blown, the judgment shifts to the heavens: "a third of the sun was struck, a third of the moon, and a third of the stars, so that a third of them were darkened. A third of the day did not shine, and likewise the night" (8:12).

This mirrors the ninth plague on Egypt a tangible darkness. But here it's specific fractions: one-third less light by day, and one-third of the night with no starlight or moonlight. Perhaps the best way to imagine it literally is an eclipse-like dimming or cosmic dust covering the sky. If the previous trumpets involve volcanic ash or burning debris, the atmosphere could be so clogged that sunlight is diminished by 33%. Nighttime likewise has a strange darkness for a portion of it (maybe moon and stars obscured for a third of the night hours).

Another way to read "a third...so that a third of them were darkened" is that one-third of the day (perhaps 8 hours out of 24) had no light at all, and similarly for night meaning extended darkness periods. It's a bit unclear, but the main point is a noticeable reduction in available light. Jesus predicted signs in the sun, moon, and stars (Luke 21:25) and that "the sun will be darkened and the moon will not give its light" before His return (Matt. 24:29). This trumpet may be a precursor to the even greater darkening in the bowl judgments (Revelation 16:10 speaks of the beast's kingdom full of darkness).

Symbolically, lights in heaven represent governing authorities (as Joseph's dream in Gen. 37:9 showed sun for father, moon for mother, stars for brothers/tribes; also Isaiah 13:10 and Ezekiel 32:7 use heavenly darkening as an image of a kingdom's fall). Thus, some expositors see the 4th trumpet as signifying the collapse of government and leadership in the affected "third" of the world. William Kelly writes that this "denotes the extinction of all government within the western empire" - all the governing powers ( $(sun=$ supreme ruler, moon = lesser authority, stars = subordinate officials) are smitten [34]. In other words, anarchy or total breakdown of order ensues in that part of the world. Indeed, he calls it the symbolic presentation of that which is "wholly beyond nature" (so not a mere eclipse) [35].

Whether taken literally (cosmic calamity) or politically (government chaos) or both, the effect is a gloom and disorientation. A world already reeling from war, famine, poisoned waters, etc., now experiences eerie darkness. Think of how unnerving darkness can be people often feel fear, confusion, and despair when lights go out. Imagine the psychological impact of day suddenly turning dark by one-third: crops would suffer from lack of sunlight, temperatures might drop, and constant fear could set in.

This judgment is reminiscent of Amos 8:9, "It shall come to pass in that day...that I will make the sun go down at noon, and I will darken the earth in broad daylight." Also Joel 2:31, "The sun shall be turned into darkness and the moon into blood, before the coming of the great and awesome day of the Lord." So, this is part of the pattern marking the Day of the Lord's approach.

Notably, the first four trumpets together have affected land, sea, fresh water, and sky - the four "realms" of creation. It's as if God is systematically shaking the natural order that people idolize or depend on: the ground that produces food, the sea that provides food/trade, the rivers that give drink, and the very lights of heaven that mark time and seasons. This fulfills what Hebrews 12:26-27 says: God will shake not only earth but also heaven, so that only the unshakable (His kingdom) remains.

Personal Application:

Darkness in Scripture often represents judgment or evil prevailing (e.g., when Jesus was crucified, darkness covered the land at midday - Luke 23:44-45). Here it's a direct judgment. For us, it serves as a warning: Jesus said, "I must work the works of Him who sent Me while it is day; night is coming when no one can work" (John 9:4). If even physical light is not guaranteed, how urgent it is to make use of the "day" of God's grace to repent and serve Him. The night of judgment is coming.

Conversely, Jesus calls His disciples "the light of the world" (Matt. 5:14). Now is our time to shine through the gospel. But in that future hour, lights will go out - perhaps even the church's witness is gone (since the Church is raptured before these trumpets in the pre- trib view). How awful to be in a world with virtually no godly influence and even nature's lights dimmed! This should cultivate compassion in us to reach people now. "Walk while you have the light, lest darkness overtake you" (John 12:35).

Another reflection: consider how we react to trials. Sometimes God may allow "darkness" a period where guidance seems less, comfort is dim. Those are times to trust and seek Him by faith, not by sight. Amos 5:18 warns against desiring the Day of the Lord, "for it will be darkness, not light." Some may cavalierly think they can face judgment or doomsday. But the reality will be terrifying confusion. Only in Christ can one have light within even if darkness is without. So ensure you have the Light of life (John 8:12) in your heart, then you'll have hope even when external lights fail.

Finally, think prophetically: These cosmic disturbances lead into the last three trumpets (the "woes") which are demonic and utterly dreadful. Yet, in God's mercy, He warns with these preliminary judgments. Each increasing disaster is a call to repentance. Sadly, as we'll see later in Revelation 9:20-21, people harden their hearts. But for anyone reading or hearing these prophecies now, the message is clear: Do not delay turning to God. The present age is the brightest day; the future holds increasing darkness for a Christ-rejecting world.

Revelation 8:13 -A Solemn Warning: "Woe, Woe, Woe" to Earth's Inhabitants

After the fourth trumpet, John's attention shifts to an eagle flying in mid-heaven (high in the sky) proclaiming with a loud voice, "Woe, woe, woe to the inhabitants of the earth, because of the remaining blasts of the trumpet of the three angels who are about to sound!" (8:13). Some manuscripts say "angel" instead of eagle, but the better reading is "eagle" [36]. An eagle, known for sharp sight and swift flight, is an appropriate herald of coming woe perhaps symbolizing the swiftness of judgment (Deut. 28:49 describes an enemy as an eagle swooping down).

This flying eagle announces three "woes." In prophetic language, "woe" signifies great sorrow and terror. Each of the next three trumpets (the 5th, 6th, 7th) is called a "woe." Unlike the first four, these will directly torment humans (the first four indirectly caused human suffering through nature; the next ones explicitly target people, especially the unrepentant). The "inhabitants of the earth" (or "earth-dwellers") is a key Revelation term for the stubborn ungodly who live for the world [36]. It doesn't just mean living on earth (which includes everyone); it implies those invested in the earthly life as opposed to having their citizenship in heaven. Revelation uses this phrase repeatedly for those who resist God and persecute His people (e.g. Rev. 6:10, 13:8). So the eagle is basically saying: "If you think the first four trumpets were bad, woe to you people of earth - the worst is yet coming in the next three!"

This verse serves as a transition and a final warning. God in His mercy sends a flying messenger to shout an alarm. It's reminiscent of Ezekiel 33:11, where God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked but calls them to turn from their way. Or like Amos 3:7, "Surely the Lord GOD does nothing, unless He reveals His secret to His servants the prophets". Here, through the eagle, God reveals ahead: three horrors are about to befall mankind (we later find out: the demonic locust plague in ch.9, the demonic horsemen war in ch.9, and the final woes of ch.11-including the kingdom's announcement and bowl prelude).

Also, note the triple repetition: "Woe, woe, woe." In Scripture, repeating something thrice elevates it to the superlative (like "Holy, holy, holy"). This means the coming judgments are extremely severe - essentially worst possible woes. It might also hint there's one woe for each remaining trumpet. Indeed, Revelation 9:12 will say "One woe is past," Revelation 11:14, "the second woe is past," etc. So John is structuring the narrative clearly: trumpets $5=$ first woe, $6=$ second woe, $7=$ third woe.

The fact an eagle/angel is flying in mid-heaven crying out suggests everyone can hear this warning. Perhaps God will even use literal signs or angelic proclamations during the Tribulation (Rev. 14:6 depicts an angel preaching the gospel to earth). Even at that late hour, God is extending a hand of warning for any who would heed. It reminds us of Jesus' words: "And I looked, and I heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven, saying with a loud voice..." (this very verse). We might recall Jesus saying, "as the lightning comes from the east and flashes to the west, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be" (Matt. 24:27) an image of something visible to all. Possibly this eagle is a divine lightning flash of warning across the sky.

Personal Application:

This ominous "Woe, woe, woe" teaches that God's judgments increase for the persistently unrepentant. Romans 2:5 warns those who harden their hearts that they are treasuring up wrath for themselves. We see that progression here: despite 4 trumpets, many remain earth-dwellers in love with sin, so greater woes come. Let's examine our hearts: is there any "earth-dweller" mentality in us? (Love of the world, neglect of God's Word, refusal to repent of a known sin.) If so, we must heed the warning now. Hebrews 12:25: "See that you do not refuse Him who speaks... for if those on earth did not escape... much more shall we not escape if we turn away from Him who speaks from heaven." The eagle's cry is basically "escape these things by turning to God!"

For believers, there's also encouragement: The fact God announces woes means He's in control. Nothing happens by accident. Even the devilish torments to come are within God's plan and timing. We can trust that God knows how to distinguish "earth-dwellers" from His own. In Revelation 9, the locusts only hurt those without God's seal. So, God knows those who are His and keeps them (2 Tim. 2:19). It's a comfort to know our loving Father warns before discipline or judgment. Are we listening to His warnings in our lives? Perhaps not an eagle voice, but the Holy Spirit's conviction, the counsel of Christian friends, the clear teaching of Scripture - these often warn us when we veer toward sin. Let's not be like those who ignore warnings until calamity strikes.

Another reflection: Do we have God's perspective on the world? The eagle said "Woe to the inhabitants of the earth." If we truly believe what Scripture says about the fate of this present world system, we should pity and evangelize those who are living only for this life. James 5:1 echoes, "Come now, you rich, weep and howl for your miseries that are coming upon you!" The world chases money, pleasure, power, but we know "woe" is coming on all that. Let's thus live for what's eternal storing treasure in heaven, not on earth - and urgently call others to do the same, before the final woes arrive.

Conclusion: Key Insights and Personal Lessons from Revelation 7-8

In these two chapters, we have seen a panorama of God's grace and justice in the end times. Chapter 7 highlighted God's grace: even as judgments pause, He seals 144,000 Israelites, a testament to His covenant faithfulness, and He saves a countless multitude from the nations, a testament to His global mercy. Chapter 8 then plunged us back into His justice: the seventh seal introduced escalating judgments (the trumpets) that systematically strike the earth, sea, waters, and sky - showing God's holy wrath against sin and idolatry.

From a doctrinal standpoint, this passage reinforces a dispensational understanding of God's plan. The Church (redeemed already in heaven, symbolized by the elders) is distinct from Israel (the 144,000 sealed servants) and from the saved Gentile nations (the great multitude) [10] [12]. God has a role for each in the climax of history, yet all are saved by the blood of the Lamb and all ultimately join in His praise [16]. We see the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies: Israel's preserved remnant, Gentiles worshiping in the Kingdom, creation suffering under plagues akin to Egypt's, and cosmic signs preceding the Day of the Lord. It's a vivid confirmation that "the Scripture cannot be broken" [37] every promise and warning God has given will come to pass.

For personal spiritual life, these chapters are rich with application:

  • Worship and Hope: The vision of the multinational multitude worshiping reminds us that our destiny as believers is to worship God in joy forever. No trial can prevent that these people came out of great tribulation, yet ended up singing and serving. So we should be a worshiping people now, characterized by gratitude for salvation. Our hope is not in worldly peace (which will be shattered in tribulation) but in the Lamb who is our Shepherd. He will guide us to living water and wipe away every tear. Cling to that hope in your hardships; let it purify you (1 John 3:3).
  • Faithfulness and Sealing: The 144,000, though a future specific group, inspire us to be faithful servants marked by God. Today, we are sealed by the Spirit - do we live in a manner that reflects God's ownership? They were kept pure in a vile world (Rev. 14:4-5 indicates their character). Likewise, we are called to holiness amidst a crooked generation. God knows how to protect His own; our part is to follow Him wholeheartedly.
  • The Power of Prayer: The heavenly incense scene teaches that our prayers matter. They ascend before God's throne and in His timing they precipitate world-changing events. This encourages us not to lose heart in prayer (Luke 18:1). Every "Thy kingdom come" we pray is stored in heaven. One day, perhaps soon, the fire will fall and Christ will come to set things right, in direct response to those prayers. So pray on- especially for the lost, for justice, for God's glory. And realize prayer is a serious partnership with God: praying for His kingdom means also praying for judgment on evil. Are we prepared for Him to answer?
  • Urgency of the Gospel: Knowing what judgments are coming upon the earth- dwellers, we should feel a burning urgency to share the gospel now, in this age of grace. "Behold, now is the accepted time; behold, now is the day of salvation" (2 Cor. 6:2). Revelation 7 shows many will be saved during the Tribulation, but they will pay a heavy price (hunger, persecution, etc.). Far better to turn to Christ now and be part of the Bride taken to heaven before these woes. Let's use the relatively peaceful "daylight" we have to witness, lest loved ones be left to face these horrors. The eagle's triple "Woe" warns us that time is short and wrath is certain a motivation for evangelism and holy living (2 Peter 3:11-12).
  • Trust in God's Sovereignty: In these chapters, not a single event is out of God's control. Angels hold winds, another seals saints, trumpets are given by God, even the precise fractions $(1/3)$ show God's measured restraint. This should bolster our confidence in God's sovereignty over today's events too. The world may seem chaotic, but God has an exact timeline and plan. We can trust Him through our personal "tribulations," knowing He ordains limits to them and purposes in them. If He can protect 144,000 through the tribulation, He can guard our souls through any trial (2 Tim. 1:12).
  • Sobriety and Repentance: Finally, these chapters give a sober reminder of the seriousness of sin. Look at what it leads to a world on fire, waters poisoned, heavens darkened, people tormented and dying. God's judgments are called His "strange work" (Isaiah 28:21), but they are a necessary work to purge evil. Don't flirt with the very sins that will bring about such devastation. As believers, we are not appointed to wrath (1 Thess. 5:9), but we are called to live soberly, watching and praying. If anyone reading this has not fully yielded to Christ, now is the time. These prophecies are given not to satisfy curiosity, but to drive us to decision. Mercy is available now - Jesus, the Lamb of God, offers cleansing by His blood (Rev. 7:14). But wrath is coming on those who dwell on the earth. The double image of Christ as Lamb and Judge in these chapters should lead us to either run to Him for salvation or, if we are saved, to bow in reverence and say, "Who is like You, O Lord!".

In conclusion, Revelation 7 and 8 reveal a God who saves a multitude no one can number and also a God who punishes a world that refuses Him. This twin reality-salvation and judgment-flows from God's holy love. As A.W. Tozer once said, "God's justice and mercy meet at the cross." We see that those who wash their robes in the Lamb's blood experience mercy (7:14-17), while those who harden their hearts face escalating judgment (8:7-13). Which side of the cross are we on? That is the crucial question. For those of us who answer, "By grace, on the side of the Lamb," these chapters cause us to rejoice in so great a salvation and motivate us to live for His glory. For those unsure or still clinging to this world: hear the eagle's cry and the Spirit's voice. Repent, believe in Christ, and "flee from the wrath to come" - He will graciously receive you now.

"Even so, come, Lord Jesus!" Amen.
